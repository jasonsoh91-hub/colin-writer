You don't really need seventeen types of vinegar to cook a decent meal. But you do need a few things on hand that'll get you through most situations without having to dash to the shop every time inspiration strikes.

A well-stocked pantry isn't about hoarding every ingredient that might one day be useful. It's about having the building blocks that let you improvise when the fridge is looking sparse, or when you've promised someone dinner and realised you have no plan whatsoever. These are the staples that earn their keep.

Here are three that'll actually see regular use.

Good quality soy sauce

At first glance, soy sauce is just salty brown liquid. But once you start paying attention, the differences between a proper aged soy sauce and the stuff that comes in a plastic bottle shaped like a fish become impossible to ignore.

A decent soy sauce should taste complex—savoury, yes, but with layers underneath. Something fermented and round, not just aggressively salty. You'll want it for marinades, stir-fries, and that moment when a soup needs depth but you can't quite figure out what's missing. The answer is usually soy sauce. I've used it to rescue bland pasta sauces more times than I'd care to admit, and it works because good soy sauce has umami that doesn't announce itself, it just makes everything taste more like itself.

There are light and dark varieties. Light soy sauce is thinner and saltier, better for seasoning. Dark soy sauce is thicker, slightly sweet, and adds colour to braises and fried rice. If you're only keeping one, go with light—it's more versatile. But if you've got the space, both will serve you well. Kikkoman is a safe bet and available practically everywhere, from Jaya Grocer to your neighbourhood sundry shop. If you want to go a step further, look for naturally brewed versions—they'll say so on the label, and the ingredient list should be short: soybeans, wheat, salt, water.

The cheap stuff tastes like somebody dissolved a stock cube in seawater and called it a day. You'll notice the difference the first time you use a proper bottle, and after that, going back feels like a downgrade you can't justify.

Neutral cooking oil

This might sound boring, but hear me out. You need an oil that doesn't have opinions about what you're cooking. Something that can handle high heat without smoking out your kitchen or adding flavours you didn't ask for.

Vegetable oil, canola oil, sunflower oil—they all do the job. The point is to have something neutral that works for frying, sautéing, roasting, and the occasional deep-fry when you're feeling ambitious (or reckless, depending on how you look at it). Olive oil is lovely, but it's not what you want when you're searing chicken thighs at high heat or making a batch of fried shallots. It'll burn, it'll taste bitter, and you'll wonder why things went wrong. That's where your neutral oil comes in.

Picture this: you're halfway through a recipe that calls for frying something until golden and crispy. You reach for the olive oil because it's the only thing in the cupboard. The smoke alarm goes off. Your housemate is annoyed. The chicken is unevenly cooked and vaguely tastes like disappointment. A bottle of neutral oil would've prevented all of that.

Keep a large bottle somewhere accessible. It doesn't need to be fancy, but it does need to exist. And if you're the sort of person who also keeps a small bottle of toasted sesame oil around for finishing dishes, good on you—that's the kind of forethought that separates a functioning kitchen from one that's just winging it.

Dried pasta

I know, I know—pasta feels almost too obvious to mention. But it's here for a reason, and that reason is that dried pasta is the most forgiving ingredient you can own. It doesn't go off. It cooks in ten minutes. It pairs with practically anything you can think of, from a tin of tuna to leftover roast chicken to that jar of sambal you impulse-bought and haven't opened yet.

The trick is to have a couple of shapes on hand. Long pasta like spaghetti or linguine is good for quick tosses with garlic, oil, and whatever vegetables you've got lying around. Short pasta like penne or fusilli holds onto sauces better and works for baked dishes or cold pasta salads when the weather's too hot to think straight. I'm not saying you need an entire shelf focused to pasta shapes, but two types will cover most scenarios without requiring a flowchart to decide which one to use.

Sometimes better, if we're being honest, because it has more bite and doesn't turn to mush if you leave it in the water an extra thirty seconds while you answer the door. Barilla, San Remo, or even the supermarket own-brand stuff—they all work. Just make sure you salt the water generously (it should taste like the sea, or at least like a moderately salty version of it), and don't overcook it. Al dente isn't just a fancy term; it's the point where pasta still has some backbone and doesn't cling to your fork like it's given up on life.

The next time you're staring into an empty fridge wondering what to make for dinner, a box of dried pasta and a few other staples will get you further than you think. That has to count for something.
