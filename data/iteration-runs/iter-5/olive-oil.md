You probably think you know what olive oil is. A bottle on the counter, something you drizzle over salads, maybe something you've been told is good for you. In practice, though, it's one of the most misunderstood ingredients in most kitchens, and the differences between a RM15 bottle and a RM80 one are far more significant than most people realise.

Olive oil has been around for thousands of years, which means humanity has had plenty of time to figure out what works and what doesn't. The Mediterranean has been pressing olives since before anyone thought to write any of this down, and what they've arrived at is a system that separates the exceptional from the merely acceptable. The problem is that modern supermarket shelves have made it harder, not easier, to tell the difference.

Extra virgin olive oil is where the conversation starts, and it's the only one that really matters if you're after flavour. It's the first cold pressing of olives, extracted without heat or chemicals, which means everything that makes olives interesting stays in the bottle. You'll find it labelled with all manner of descriptors that sound like wine tasting—grassy, peppery, fruity, robust—and unlike most marketing speak, these actually mean something. A good extra virgin will have a sharp, almost aggressive quality at the back of your throat, a kind of peppery bite that makes you cough if you're not expecting it. That's not a defect; that's polyphenols, the compounds responsible for both the flavour and the health benefits people keep going on about.

Then there's regular olive oil, sometimes called pure or light, which is what happens when you take lower-grade olives or the leftovers from the first pressing and refine them with heat and chemicals until they're neutral enough to not offend anyone. It's cheaper, it's milder, and it's what you reach for when you need to fry something at a higher temperature without setting off the smoke alarm. It won't give you much in the way of flavour, but it also won't cost you half your grocery budget, which, depending on your priorities, might be a fair trade.

Spanish olive oil tends to be bold and fruity, the kind of thing that makes itself known the moment it hits the pan. Spain produces more olive oil than any other country, and they've turned it into something of a national identity, with regions like Andalucía churning out bottles that range from the everyday to the sort of thing you'd hesitate to use for anything less than a special occasion. Italian olive oil, on the other hand, often skews more delicate, with a grassy, herbaceous quality that works particularly well when you're after something that whispers rather than shouts.

Greek olive oil doesn't get nearly as much attention as it deserves, which is a shame, because it's some of the best in the world. The Koroneiki olive, grown primarily in the Peloponnese and Crete, produces an oil that's peppery, robust, and unapologetically assertive. It's the kind of thing that makes you rethink what olive oil is supposed to taste like, and if you've only ever had the mass-market stuff, it's worth tracking down a bottle just to see what you've been missing. You'll find it at Jaya Grocer or Ben's Independent Grocer if you're in KL, and it's usually priced somewhere between the cheap and the extortionate, which feels about right.

Portuguese olive oil is another one that flies under the radar, and much like the country itself, it doesn't make a fuss about being excellent. The Alentejo region produces oils that are smooth, slightly sweet, and less aggressive than their Spanish or Greek counterparts, which makes them particularly good for finishing dishes where you want the oil to add richness without dominating everything else on the plate. If you're the sort of person who likes to drizzle olive oil over grilled fish or roasted vegetables, this is the one to reach for.

Of course, there's also the issue of freshness, which is something most people don't think about until it's too late. Olive oil doesn't age like wine; it degrades. Light, heat, and oxygen are its enemies, and if you've had the same bottle sitting on your counter for the past year, you're essentially cooking with something that's gone rancid without you noticing. A fresh bottle should smell alive, almost green, with a sharpness that makes you want to taste it straight from the spoon (and you should, at least once, just to see what good oil is supposed to be like). If it smells flat, musty, or vaguely like crayons, it's done for, and no amount of drizzling is going to save your salad.

I haven't even touched on the business of PDO and PGI labels, or the fact that some producers are so particular about their olives that they'll harvest them by hand at dawn to avoid bruising, or the way certain bottles are wrapped in dark glass or tin to protect the oil from light, all of which would require an entire other article if we're being honest. There's a lot more to say, but I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book.

If you've been treating olive oil as an afterthought, something you grab without looking because they're all basically the same, it might be worth spending a bit more on a bottle that actually tastes like something. Find a good extra virgin, preferably one that lists the harvest date and the region it's from, and use it the way it's meant to be used—sparingly, deliberately, and with the kind of respect you'd give any other ingredient that's been perfected over millennia. You'll notice the difference, and once you do, it's hard to go back.
