You don't really need to think about olive oil this much. But once you start, it becomes difficult to go back to treating it as just another bottle of fat sitting on the shelf. Walk into any well-stocked grocery store and you'll find yourself faced with an entire wall of olive oil, each bottle making increasingly elaborate claims about its provenance, its harvest date, its cold-pressed purity, and why it deserves to cost three times as much as the one sitting next to it. Some of these claims are marketing. Others are the real deal. The challenge is knowing which is which.

Olive oil has been around for thousands of years, which means humanity has had plenty of time to complicate what should be a relatively straightforward process of crushing olives and collecting what comes out. The ancient Greeks and Romans were already obsessed with it, using it for everything from cooking to lamp fuel to rubbing all over themselves before athletic competitions, which must have made for some interesting wrestling matches. Fast forward a few millennia and we've managed to turn it into an industry with more grades, labels, and regulatory loopholes than anyone asked for.

The first thing to understand is that not all olive oil is created equal, and the difference isn't just about price. 8%. This is the stuff you want for finishing dishes, drizzling over salads, or dipping bread into while pretending you're not going to eat the entire loaf. Virgin olive oil is a step down, with slightly higher acidity and less pronounced flavour. Then you have regular olive oil, sometimes labelled as "pure" olive oil, which is a blend of refined olive oil and virgin olive oil. It's perfectly fine for cooking at higher temperatures, but if you're using it as a finishing oil, you're missing the point entirely.

Spain produces more olive oil than any other country, accounting for nearly half of the world's supply. Spanish olive oil tends to be robust and fruity, with varieties like Picual offering a peppery bite that catches at the back of your throat in a way that's oddly satisfying. Italy comes in second, and Italian olive oil often leans towards a more delicate, grassy profile, though this is a gross simplification given that Italy has over 500 olive varieties. Tuscan oils are known for their peppery finish, while Ligurian oils are softer and more buttery. Greece produces some of the oldest olive oil in the world, with Kalamata olives yielding a smooth, fruity oil that's less aggressive than its Spanish counterparts but no less compelling.

Then we have the harvest date, which is something most people don't even think to check. Olive oil is not wine; it doesn't improve with age. Fresh olive oil, ideally consumed within a year or two of harvest, has a brightness and complexity that fades over time. The best producers will stamp the harvest date on the bottle, and if you can't find one, that should tell you something. You'll sometimes find oils labelled as "early harvest", which means the olives were picked while still green and unripe. This produces an oil with more polyphenols, those antioxidants everyone's always going on about, and a more intense, slightly bitter flavour that some people love and others find too aggressive.

Storage matters more than you'd think. Light and heat are olive oil's enemies, which is why the good stuff comes in dark glass bottles or tins. If you're buying olive oil in a clear bottle that's been sitting on a sunny shelf for months, you're essentially buying expensive vegetable oil at that point. Keep it in a cool, dark place, and for the love of all that's holy, don't store it next to the stove where it's subjected to constant temperature fluctuations. I've seen people do this, and it becomes an expensive chew toy that even the dog thinks is not worth the bother.

The whole "cooking with extra virgin olive oil" debate is one of those things that refuses to die, despite the science being fairly settled. Yes, you can cook with extra virgin olive oil. Its smoke point is around 190-210°C, which is more than adequate for most home cooking. The myth that it becomes toxic when heated is just that—a myth. Having said that, if you're deep-frying something or searing a steak at nuclear temperatures, you might want to reach for a more refined oil simply because you're not going to taste the nuances of that expensive Tuscan bottle anyway. Save the good stuff for when it can actually shine.

There's also the matter of fraud, which sounds dramatic but is a genuine issue in the olive oil industry. Some bottles labelled as extra virgin olive oil are cut with cheaper refined oils, or they're made from olives that are past their prime and shouldn't qualify for the designation. The Italian government has had to crack down on this repeatedly, and even now, there's no foolproof way to guarantee what you're getting unless you're buying from a producer you trust. If the price seems too good to be true, it probably is. A decent bottle of extra virgin olive oil should cost at least RM40-50 for 500ml, and the really good stuff will run you considerably more.

I haven't even touched on flavoured olive oils, single-estate bottlings, or the various PDO and PGI designations that exist across Europe, but if I did, I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book. The point is that olive oil is one of those ingredients where paying attention actually makes a difference. You don't need to become an olive oil sommelier, but understanding the basics means you'll stop wasting money on mediocre bottles and start appreciating what a good oil can do for a simple dish.

If you've been treating olive oil as an afterthought, something you buy once a year and leave sitting in the cupboard until it goes rancid, it might be time to reconsider. Start with one good bottle of extra virgin olive oil, check the harvest date, store it properly, and use it the way it was meant to be used—as a final flourish, a bit of richness drizzled over something that deserves it. You'll taste the difference, and once you do, it's hard to go back to not caring.
