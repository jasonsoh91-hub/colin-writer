You don't need a pantry that looks like a specialty grocer's back room. You need about seven things that work hard, stay good, and don't make you feel like an idiot when you open the cupboard at 7pm on a Wednesday. Here are three that'll actually earn their shelf space.

Good olive oil

At first glance, olive oil is just the thing you drizzle on salads or use to stop pasta from clumping together. But if you've ever tasted the difference between a bottle that cost eight ringgit and one that cost forty, you'll know there's a gap wide enough to drive a lorry through. The cheap stuff is fine for frying onions or greasing a baking tin, but when you're finishing a dish—drizzling it over grilled fish, dressing tomatoes, or just dipping bread into it because you're too hungry to wait for dinner—that's when a proper olive oil makes you look like you know what you're doing. Look for something labelled extra virgin, ideally from a single country (Italy, Spain, Greece), and stored in a dark bottle or tin. It should taste grassy, peppery, maybe slightly bitter—not neutral, not bland. Once you've got it, keep it away from the stove. Heat and light are olive oil's natural enemies, which means that bottle sitting next to your cooktop in full sunlight is slowly turning into expensive cooking oil with none of the character left. Store it in a cupboard, use it generously on anything that doesn't require high heat, and save the supermarket stuff for everything else. The next time you're making something as simple as pasta aglio e olio, you'll notice it. Your friends will notice it. That has to count for something.

Soy sauce (the real kind)

Despite what the label on some bottles would have you believe, not all soy sauce is created equal. There's the stuff that tastes like it was designed in a lab to approximate the idea of soy sauce, and then there's the kind that's been brewed the old-fashioned way—fermented slowly, with nothing but soybeans, wheat, salt, and time. The difference is the same as the one between instant coffee and the real thing, except somehow more obvious once you've tried both. A naturally brewed soy sauce has depth. It's salty, yes, but there's also a kind of umami richness that makes everything from fried rice to a simple cucumber salad taste like you put in more effort than you actually did. Kikkoman is the easy answer here—widely available, reliable, and brewed properly. If you can find it, a bottle of Indonesian kecap manis is worth having around too, though that's technically a different thing entirely (sweet, thick, molasses-dark). Picture this: you've just stir-fried some vegetables and they taste fine but not great, a little flat, a little forgettable. A tablespoon of good soy sauce in the final thirty seconds changes the entire equation. It doesn't fix bad cooking, but it does make decent cooking taste like you knew what you were aiming for all along. Keep it in the fridge after opening if you're the sort of person who buys a large bottle and then forgets about it for six months. Otherwise, just keep it in the cupboard and use it often enough that it doesn't have time to go off.

Dried pasta

You'd think pasta is pasta, but spend five minutes comparing a box of De Cecco to the cheapest thing on the shelf and you'll see what I'm talking about. The good stuff holds its shape when you cook it, doesn't turn to mush if you leave it in the water an extra minute, and has a texture that actually grips the sauce instead of letting it slide off like water on a windshield. Dried pasta isn't a compromise or a backup plan—it's what most Italians use at home, and they're not exactly known for cutting corners when it comes to food. Look for something made with bronze dies, which gives the surface a slightly rough texture. It costs a bit more, maybe twelve or fifteen ringgit instead of six, but it's the difference between pasta that tastes like you care and pasta that tastes like you were just trying to fill the plate. Spaghetti, penne, or rigatoni will cover most situations. Keep a couple of shapes around and you've got the foundation for a quick dinner whenever you can't be bothered to think too hard. Boil it in heavily salted water (as salty as the sea, they say, though I've never actually tasted seawater to confirm), drain it a minute before the packet says it's done, and finish it in the pan with whatever sauce you've got going. The pasta will soak up the flavour and thicken the sauce slightly as it finishes cooking, which is the sort of small detail that makes people think you know what you're doing even if you're just winging it.

The next time you're standing in the supermarket aisle wondering if you really need that bottle of truffle oil or that tin of saffron threads, consider whether you've got these three sorted first. Chances are they'll do more for your cooking than anything with a fancy label and a price tag that makes you wince.
