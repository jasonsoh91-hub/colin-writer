You don't really need to think about salt this much. But once you start, it becomes impossible to ignore how much work it's doing while pretending to be simple. Walk into any supermarket and you'll find yourself staring at an entire shelf of what is, chemically speaking, the same compound arranged in different shapes and sold at wildly different prices. There's the standard table salt in the blue container that's been in your grandmother's kitchen since before you were born, flaky sea salt that costs more per gram than some cuts of meat, and pink Himalayan salt being marketed like it fell from some mystical mountain peak where enlightenment grows on trees. And the thing is, some of these differences actually matter.

Salt's place in cooking goes back far enough that trying to pinpoint an origin feels about as useful as asking when humans first figured out that fire was warm. What we do know is that salt was valuable enough to be used as currency in some parts of the world, and that entire trade routes were built around moving it from place to place. The Romans paid soldiers partly in salt—hence "salary"—and in certain parts of Africa, salt was traded weight-for-weight with gold. People have fought wars over it, taxed it, smuggled it, and generally treated it like the extremely powerful chemical seasoning that it is, long before anyone could explain why it makes food taste better.

Table salt is where most people start, and there's no shame in that. It's sodium chloride that's been mined, refined, and ground down until it's as fine as sand, with iodine added to prevent thyroid problems in populations that might not get enough of it otherwise. The iodisation programme that started in the 1920s is genuinely one of public health's quiet success stories, which doesn't get talked about much because nobody writes songs about goitre prevention. Table salt dissolves fast, measures consistently, and costs almost nothing. If you're baking and a recipe calls for salt without specifying which kind, this is probably what they mean. The grains are uniform, which makes it reliable. It's also, shall we say, not particularly interesting to look at.

Sea salt is what happens when you let ocean water evaporate and scrape up what's left behind. Unlike table salt, it hasn't been stripped of its trace minerals, which means it carries faint notes of whatever else was in that particular body of water—calcium, magnesium, potassium, and all manner of other elements that give it a slightly more complex flavour. The crystals are larger and more irregular, which means they don't dissolve as quickly and can add a bit of texture when used as a finishing salt. Maldon, the flaky pyramid-shaped stuff from England, has become something of a kitchen staple for people who like the idea of sprinkling something visibly crunchy over a dish just before serving. You'll find chefs using it on everything from chocolate chip cookies to grilled fish, where it provides a brief saline crunch before disappearing. It's also about ten times the price of table salt, which either feels justified or completely absurd depending on how much you care about the difference.

Then there's kosher salt, which gets its name not because it's kosher—all pure salt is kosher—but because its large, coarse grains were traditionally used in the koshering process to draw blood out of meat. The flaky structure makes it easy to pinch and sprinkle with your fingers, and because the crystals are larger, a tablespoon of kosher salt weighs less than a tablespoon of table salt. This is the kind of thing that sounds trivial until you accidentally oversalt something because you didn't account for it. Many professional kitchens default to kosher salt for this reason: it's easier to control, doesn't clump, and has a clean, straightforward salinity without the slightly metallic edge that iodised table salt can sometimes carry. Diamond Crystal and Morton are the two brands you'll see most often, and they're not interchangeable—Diamond Crystal's crystals are larger and lighter, so if a recipe specifies one and you use the other, you'll need to adjust.

Himalayan pink salt has become the sort of thing people either swear by or roll their eyes at, depending on how much they believe marketing copy about ancient mineral deposits. It comes from the Khewra Salt Mine in Pakistan, not the Himalayas, which is the first sign that we're dealing with some creative geography. The pink colour comes from trace amounts of iron oxide—rust, essentially—and while it does contain dozens of other minerals in tiny quantities, the amounts are so small that you'd need to eat an inadvisable quantity of salt to get any meaningful nutritional benefit from them. What it does have is a certain visual appeal. Those large pink crystals look striking in a grinder or piled in a bowl, and the flavour is clean and mild. Some people claim they can taste the difference. Most people probably can't, but that doesn't stop it from being a perfectly serviceable salt if you can see past the health claims and just appreciate it for what it is: sodium chloride with a nice colour.

Fleur de sel is the sort of salt that makes you feel like you're doing something fancy just by owning it. It's hand-harvested from the surface of salt ponds in France—traditionally in Guérande—where it forms delicate crystals that are skimmed off before they sink. The process is labour-intensive, the yield is small, and the price reflects that. What you get is a moist, slightly grey salt with a subtle minerality and a texture that feels almost soft. It's the kind of thing you use sparingly, sprinkled over a finished dish where you'll actually notice it: a perfectly seared steak, heirloom tomatoes, caramel, dark chocolate. Using fleur de sel to season pasta water would be like using a first-edition book as kindling. Technically possible, but missing the point entirely.

Having said that, I've left out smoked salts, black lava salt, sel gris, and all manner of other variations that would require me to turn this into something resembling an encyclopedia. The world of salt is far larger than most people give it credit for, and you could spend years exploring regional variations and traditional harvesting methods if you had that kind of time and a particularly high tolerance for sodium.

If there's one thing worth taking away from all this, it's that salt isn't just salt in the same way that butter isn't just butter. The differences might be subtle, but they're there, and once you start paying attention, it's hard to go back to treating it like a background ingredient that just exists. Whether you stick with the blue container or start collecting artisanal finishing salts from different coastlines is entirely up to you. But at least now you'll know what you're looking at when you find yourself standing in that supermarket aisle, staring at a dozen versions of the same chemical compound and wondering if it really matters which one you choose. Sometimes it does. Sometimes it doesn't. That has to count for something.
