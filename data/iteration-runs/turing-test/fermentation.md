Fermentation isn't magic. It's just rot we decided to put in jars and call fancy. There, I said it.

You're probably looking at me like I've lost my mind, pointing to your jar of kimchi or that sourdough starter you've named Gerald and insisting there's more to it than that. And sure, there's a difference between a cabbage that's gone bad in the back of your fridge and one that's been deliberately transformed into something tangy and preserved. But that difference isn't some mystical alchemy—it's just controlled decay with better PR and a salt content high enough to keep the wrong microbes from crashing the party.

Take kimchi, for instance. You salt some cabbage, pack it into a jar, and leave it on your counter for a few days. What's happening in there is that Lactobacillus bacteria—the same stuff that lives in your gut, shall we say, harmoniously—are eating the sugars in the vegetables and producing lactic acid as waste. That acid drops the pH low enough that the bacteria you don't want (the ones that would turn your cabbage into a science experiment gone wrong) can't survive. It's not preservation through technique. It's preservation through making the environment so inhospitable that only the good microbes stick around. The Koreans figured this out centuries before anyone knew what bacteria were, which is either impressive or slightly concerning depending on how you feel about trial and error in food safety.

Or consider beer, which is just controlled spoilage with carbonation. You take grain, soak it until it starts to sprout, dry it out, crush it, boil it with hops, and then introduce yeast that converts the sugars into alcohol and carbon dioxide. The yeast—usually Saccharomyces cerevisiae if you're brewing ales, or Saccharomyces pastorianus for lagers—is doing exactly what it would do in the wild, which is eat sugar and excrete alcohol as a byproduct. We've just learned to time it, control the temperature, and bottle the results before it turns into vinegar. Every beer you've ever had is the result of billions of microscopic organisms getting drunk on malt and dying in the process. That's not poetry. That's just biology with better branding.

Then there's cheese, which starts as milk and ends as something that can age for years without refrigeration. You add bacteria or mould (Penicillium roqueforti for blue cheese, Propionibacterium freudenreichii for the holes in Swiss), and they break down the lactose and proteins into smaller compounds that taste complex and nutty instead of, well, like milk. Some cheeses are aged in caves where the humidity and temperature stay consistent year-round. Others are washed in brine or alcohol to encourage specific bacteria to grow on the rind. The process is so specific that certain cheeses can only be made in certain regions because the local microbes are part of the flavour profile. Roquefort legally has to be aged in the caves of Roquefort-sur-Soulzon in France. Not because of tradition, but because the Penicillium roqueforti that lives in those caves is different from the stuff anywhere else. You're not eating cheese. You're eating geography.

Even soy sauce—the bottle sitting in your kitchen right now—is fermented. You take soybeans, mix them with roasted wheat, introduce Aspergillus oryzae (a mould), let it grow for a few days, then pack the whole thing into brine and leave it for months or years. The mould breaks down the proteins into amino acids, which is what gives soy sauce that deep umami flavour. The cheap stuff you get in plastic packets is often made with hydrolysed vegetable protein and caramel colouring, skipping the fermentation entirely, which is why it tastes flat and one-dimensional. The real thing—something like Kikkoman or, if you're feeling spendy, a barrel-aged tamari—has layers of flavour that only come from letting microbes do their work over time. It's not a shortcut. It's the whole point.

What all of this points to is that fermentation isn't a technique we invented. It's a process we borrowed from nature and learned to control just enough to make it useful. The bacteria and yeast were already breaking down sugars and producing acids and alcohol long before humans showed up. We just started putting it in jars and calling it food. The real skill isn't in the fermentation itself—it's in creating the conditions where the right organisms thrive and the wrong ones don't. Salt, temperature, time, and sometimes a little bit of luck. That's the whole toolkit.

And yet, we've built entire industries around it. Entire cuisines. You walk into any kopitiam in Malaysia and someone's drinking kopi-o with a side of roti canai, both of which involve fermentation at some point in their production. The coffee beans were fermented after being picked to remove the fruit and develop flavour. We've been doing this so long that we don't even think about it anymore. Fermentation is just what you do when you want food to last longer or taste better or both.

So no, there's no magic in your jar of kimchi. There's Lactobacillus and salt and time. And that's fine. Better than fine, actually. Because once you realise that fermentation is just controlled decay, you stop being intimidated by it. You stop thinking you need special equipment or a degree in microbiology to make your own sauerkraut or pickle some vegetables or start a sourdough starter (and yes, you can name it Gerald if you want, though the bacteria probably won't care). All you need is a clean jar, some salt, and the patience to let the microbes do what they've been doing for millions of years. That has to count for something.
