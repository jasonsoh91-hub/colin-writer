You don't really need to think about chocolate this much. But once you start, it becomes difficult to go back to treating it like just another sweet thing you grab at the checkout counter. Walk into any decent grocery store and you'll find yourself staring at an entire wall of chocolate bars, each one making claims about percentages, origins, and processing methods that sound vaguely important but somehow never quite explain what you're supposed to be tasting. The difference between a RM5 bar and a RM30 bar isn't always obvious until you've actually tried both, and by then you've spent RM35 wondering if your palate is sophisticated enough to justify the expense.

Chocolate starts as cacao pods, which grow on trees that are notoriously particular about where they're willing to live. They want heat, humidity, and shade, which is why you'll find them clustered around the equator in places like West Africa, Ecuador, and parts of Southeast Asia. The pods get cracked open to reveal beans covered in a sweet, mucilaginous pulp that ferments for several days before the beans are dried, roasted, and ground into a paste. That paste is chocolate in its most fundamental form, before anyone's added sugar, milk, or vanilla to make it more agreeable to the average person's expectations of what chocolate should taste like.

Dark chocolate is what you get when you take that paste, add some sugar, and leave out the milk. The percentage you see on the wrapper refers to how much of the bar comes from cacao—the rest is sugar and maybe a bit of vanilla or lecithin to smooth things out. A 70% bar has more cacao and less sugar than a 50% bar, which means it's more bitter, more intense, and harder to eat an entire block of in one sitting (though people certainly try). There's a sweet spot somewhere between 60% and 75% where the bitterness and the sweetness balance each other out in a way that feels almost architectural, like somebody sat down and did the maths to figure out exactly how much pleasure a single square could deliver before it became too much.

Milk chocolate takes that same paste and adds milk powder, which softens everything and makes it taste like the chocolate you probably grew up with. It's sweeter, creamier, and a lot easier to eat without pausing to consider whether you're supposed to be detecting notes of red fruit or tobacco or whatever else the tasting notes on the back of the package are trying to convince you exists. Cadbury, Hershey's, and a dozen other brands have built empires on this version of chocolate, the kind that doesn't ask much of you beyond enjoying it. There's a reason it's the default.

White chocolate is technically not chocolate at all, since it contains no cacao solids—just cacao butter, sugar, and milk. This makes it the subject of endless debate among people who take chocolate seriously, though most of them will grudgingly admit that a good white chocolate has its place, particularly when it's been made with actual cacao butter rather than vegetable oil. The best white chocolate tastes like cream that's been sweetened and solidified, with a faint floral note that comes from the cacao butter itself. The worst white chocolate tastes like sweetened wax, which is probably because it is sweetened wax, or close enough to it that the distinction doesn't matter much.

Malaysian chocolate deserves its own mention here, particularly the stuff coming out of Sabah and Sarawak, where cacao has been growing quietly for years while everyone was paying attention to beans from Ecuador and Madagascar. The climate is right, the soil is right, and the people who've been working with it have figured out how to ferment and dry it in ways that bring out fruity, floral notes that feel distinctly Southeast Asian. You'll find local chocolate makers like Chocolate Concierge and Cocoraw turning these beans into bars that can hold their own against anything imported, and the fact that they're doing it here, with beans grown a few hours' drive from where you're reading this, feels like the kind of quiet achievement that should probably get more attention than it does.

Then there's the question of tempering, which is the process of heating and cooling chocolate in a very specific way so that it sets with a glossy finish and a satisfying snap when you break it. Get the temperature wrong by a few degrees and you end up with chocolate that looks dull and feels grainy, the kind that blooms with white streaks after a few weeks because the cocoa butter has separated out and crystallised on the surface. It's still edible, but it's lost that sleek, professional sheen that makes you feel like you're eating something that was made by someone who knew what they were doing. Tempering is one of those techniques that separates the casual home cook from the person who's willing to stand over a pot with a thermometer, adjusting the heat in tiny increments until the chocolate behaves itself.

I haven't even touched on single-origin bars, bean-to-bar makers, or the difference between Dutch-processed and natural cocoa powder, all of which could fill their own articles if I let them. The point isn't to become an expert overnight, or to suddenly start only buying chocolate that comes with a map of the farm where the beans were grown (though if you do, more power to you). The point is that chocolate, like butter or vinegar or any other ingredient that seems straightforward at first glance, turns out to be far more complicated once you start paying attention.

If you've been reaching for the same chocolate bar for years without thinking about it, maybe it's time to try something different. Pick up a bar with a higher percentage, or one made with beans from a specific region, or one from a Malaysian maker who's been working with local cacao. You might not taste the notes of caramel and blackcurrant that the packaging promises, but you'll probably taste something, and that has to count for something.
