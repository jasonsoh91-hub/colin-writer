You've probably walked past the pantry aisle at the supermarket and wondered if you actually need that jar of preserved lemons or that bottle of fish sauce you saw in a recipe once. The answer is probably no. What you do need, though, is a small handful of ingredients that'll carry you through most of what you'd ever want to cook at home—things that don't go off in a week, don't require a PhD to understand, and quietly do a lot of the heavy lifting without asking for credit. Here are three that'll really make cooking a breeze.

Good olive oil

At first glance, olive oil is just another bottle of fat sitting on your shelf. In practice, though, it's the thing that makes or breaks half the dishes you'll ever cook. A decent olive oil doesn't need to cost as much as a small car, but it does need to taste like something other than vaguely greasy air. You want one that has a bit of grassiness to it, maybe a peppery bite at the back of your throat when you taste it straight (which you should, at least once, just to see what you're working with). The sort you'd be happy to drizzle over a salad or a plate of pasta without needing to drown it in other things first.

Picture this: you've just boiled some spaghetti, and you're standing there wondering how to make it taste like an actual meal rather than something a university student would eat out of desperation. A good glug of olive oil, some garlic you've sliced thin, maybe a pinch of chilli flakes if you're feeling adventurous, and suddenly you've got cacio e pepe's more approachable cousin. It clings to the pasta in a way that sunflower oil never will, coating each strand with a kind of richness that feels almost indulgent. That has to count for something.

Yes, you could use the cheapest bottle at the shop and call it a day, but then you'd be missing the point entirely. A good olive oil isn't just cooking fat—it's flavour, it's texture, it's the difference between a dish that tastes like you tried and one that tastes like you gave up halfway through. Keep one bottle for cooking, another for finishing, and you'll wonder how you ever managed without it.

Soy sauce

Despite what the label might suggest, soy sauce isn't just for stir-fries and sushi. It's one of those deceptively simple ingredients that does far more than you'd think, adding depth and a kind of savoury backbone to dishes that would otherwise taste a bit flat. You've probably seen it used in marinades or as a dipping sauce, but it works just as well in a pot of bolognese or stirred into a pan of mushrooms as it does in fried rice. The Japanese have a word for it—umami—and while that's become one of those terms people throw around to sound knowledgeable, it's also genuinely useful here. Soy sauce delivers that elusive fifth taste, the one that makes everything else taste more like itself.

There are two main types you'll come across: light soy sauce, which is saltier and thinner, and dark soy sauce, which is thicker, slightly sweet, and adds a deep caramel colour to whatever you're cooking. For most purposes, you'll want the light version. It's the workhorse, the one you'll reach for when you need to season something or add a bit of complexity without turning the whole dish brown. Dark soy sauce has its place too—particularly in braises or when you want that glossy, almost lacquered finish on roasted meats—but it's not the one you'll use every day.

The beauty of soy sauce is that it's almost impossible to mess up. A splash here, a spoonful there, and suddenly your scrambled eggs taste like something worth getting out of bed for (hopefully not literally, though I wouldn't judge). It keeps for months, doesn't ask for much attention, and quietly makes everything better. If that's not the definition of a pantry staple, I don't know what is.

Tinned tomatoes

Tinned tomatoes might just be the most underrated ingredient in your kitchen. They sit there on the shelf, unassuming and slightly dented from being shoved to the back one too many times, and yet they're the foundation of more meals than you'd care to count. Pasta sauce, curry base, shakshuka, soup—all of them start with a tin of tomatoes and a bit of faith that things will come together. And they do, almost every time, because tinned tomatoes are one of those rare ingredients that are actually better than their fresh counterparts for most cooking purposes. They're picked and processed at peak ripeness, which means they're sweeter, more consistent, and infinitely more reliable than that rock-hard supermarket tomato you optimistically bought last week.

You'll want whole peeled tomatoes rather than chopped, if you can help it. The whole ones tend to be better quality—less watery, more flavourful, and easier to control when you're cooking. You can crush them with your hands as they go into the pan (oddly satisfying, that), or leave them whole and break them down with a wooden spoon as they cook. Either way, they'll turn into a thick, rich sauce that tastes like you've been standing over the stove for hours, even if you've only been there for twenty minutes.

The thing about tinned tomatoes is that they're almost foolproof. You can throw them into a pan with some garlic and olive oil (see, it's all coming together) and have a passable marinara in the time it takes to boil pasta. Add a splash of soy sauce (there it is again) and a pinch of sugar, and you've got something that tastes like you actually know what you're doing. They don't go off, they don't ask for much, and they turn up when you need them most. That's the kind of ingredient you want in your pantry.

The next time you're standing in the supermarket aisle, staring at seventeen different varieties of exotic vinegar and wondering if you really need them all, consider this: you probably don't. What you need is a small, well-chosen arsenal of things that work hard, don't complain, and make everything else taste better. Chances are you'll spend less time shopping and more time actually cooking, which is rather the point.
