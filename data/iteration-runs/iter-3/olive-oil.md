You've probably drizzled olive oil over salad without giving it much thought. But if you've ever stood in front of a supermarket shelf staring at bottles ranging from RM15 to RM150, you'll know there's more going on here than meets the eye.

Olive oil has been around for thousands of years, which sounds like the sort of thing that gets said about every ingredient with any sort of pedigree, but in this case it's worth noting because the basic process hasn't changed all that much. You crush olives, you press them, you collect what comes out. The Phoenicians were doing it, the Romans were doing it, and now someone in Tuscany is doing it while charging you three times as much because the label has a drawing of a villa on it. What has changed, though, is how we talk about it, and more importantly, how we've managed to complicate what should be a relatively straightforward purchase.

The first thing to understand is that "extra virgin" isn't just marketing speak designed to make you feel better about spending more money. It's a technical designation that means the oil was extracted using mechanical methods only—no heat, no chemicals—and it has to meet specific standards for acidity and flavour. If an olive oil is labelled extra virgin, it should taste like olives, which sounds obvious until you realise how many bottles out there taste like nothing at all or, worse, like the inside of a cardboard box that's been left out in the rain. You'll find extra virgin olive oil from Spain, Italy, Greece, and increasingly from places like California and Australia, each with their own character shaped by climate, soil, and the particular variety of olive being pressed. Spanish oils, often made from Picual or Arbequina olives, tend to be robust and slightly peppery, the sort of thing that makes itself known when you drizzle it over tomatoes. Italian oils can range from delicate and buttery in the north to grassy and assertive in the south, and Greek oils—often from Koroneiki olives—bring a fruity brightness that works beautifully with grilled fish or a simple horiatiki.

Then there's "virgin" olive oil, which is produced the same way as extra virgin but doesn't quite meet the same standards for flavour or acidity. It's still mechanically extracted, still free of chemical processing, but it might taste a little flatter or have a slight defect that keeps it from the top tier. You're less likely to see it on shelves here, as most producers either achieve extra virgin status or drop down to the next category entirely. That next category is simply labelled "olive oil" or sometimes "pure olive oil," which is a polite way of saying it's been refined. This is oil that started out with too many flaws to be sold as virgin, so it gets treated with heat and chemicals to strip out the off-flavours, then blended with a bit of virgin oil to give it back some character. It's cheaper, it's more neutral, and it's what you'd reach for if you're frying something and don't want to spend extra virgin prices on oil that's just going to break down under high heat anyway.

There's also "light" or "extra light" olive oil, which has nothing to do with calories and everything to do with flavour. It's been refined even further to remove most of the olive taste, leaving you with something that behaves more like vegetable oil. If you're baking a cake and you don't want any interference from assertive olive notes, this is the one. But if you're buying olive oil because you actually want it to taste like olives, this isn't going to do much for you.

What complicates all of this is that olive oil, unlike wine, doesn't improve with age. In fact, it gets worse. The moment olives are pressed, the oil begins a slow decline, losing the bright, peppery notes that make good olive oil worth seeking out in the first place. That's why harvest dates matter more than you might think, and why those dusty bottles that have been sitting on a shelf for two years are probably past their best (hopefully not literally, though with olive oil you really can't be too sure). If you can find a bottle with a harvest date rather than just a best-before date, you're already ahead. Freshness is everything, which is why some of the best oils come in dark glass or tins—they're protecting the oil from light, which is one of its main enemies along with heat and oxygen.

And yet, despite all this, there's an entire world of fraudulent olive oil out there, which sounds dramatic until you realise just how common it is. You'll find bottles labelled "extra virgin" that have been cut with cheaper oils, or oils labelled "Product of Italy" that were actually produced elsewhere and just bottled in Italy, or oils that meet the legal definition of extra virgin but taste like something you'd use to lubricate a bicycle chain. It's not that all inexpensive olive oil is bad, but it does mean that price can be a useful indicator, even if it's not a guarantee. If you're paying less than RM30 for a litre of extra virgin olive oil, you should at least ask yourself why.

I haven't even touched on things like cold-pressed versus first-pressed, or the difference between PDO and PGI designations, or what it means when a bottle says "single estate" versus "blend." If I kept going, I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book. The point is that olive oil is one of those ingredients that rewards a bit of attention, even if it's just taking a moment to taste what you're buying rather than assuming all green bottles are created equal.

If you're standing in front of that shelf again, here's what I'd suggest: find a bottle with a harvest date, preferably from the current or previous year. Look for something in dark glass or a tin. Taste it if you can—it should smell fresh and fruity, maybe a little grassy, and it should have a peppery kick at the back of your throat that makes you cough slightly, which is a sign of quality, not a defect. And once you've found something you like, store it somewhere cool and dark, not next to the stove where it's going to degrade faster than your will to cook after a long day at work. Good olive oil isn't cheap, but it's one of the few ingredients that can turn a plate of roasted vegetables or a slice of sourdough into something you'd actually want to eat rather than something you're eating because you told yourself you would.
