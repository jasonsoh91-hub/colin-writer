You've probably seen olive oil described as "extra virgin" and assumed it was marketing speak, like when a phone company calls their network "ultra-premium" or a property developer labels a shoebox apartment "executive living." But it's not. Extra virgin is a technical designation, and the difference between that and regular olive oil is about as significant as the gap between fresh-squeezed orange juice and Tang.

Olive oil comes from pressing olives—hardly a revelation—but how those olives are treated determines everything that follows. Extra virgin olive oil is made from the first cold pressing of the fruit, with no heat or chemicals involved, and it has to meet strict standards for acidity and flavour. If the oil doesn't pass those tests, it gets downgraded to virgin or, worse, just "olive oil," which is often a blend of refined oil and a bit of the good stuff to give it some semblance of character. Think of it as the difference between freshly grated Parmigiano-Reggiano and that powdery substance in the green can that smells faintly of feet.

The Mediterranean is where most of the world's best olive oil comes from, and within that region, you'll find wildly different styles depending on where the olives were grown. Italian olive oil, particularly from Tuscany, tends to be robust and peppery, with a bite that can catch you off guard if you're not expecting it. It's the sort of oil that makes itself known in a dish, whether you're drizzling it over a bowl of ribollita or using it to finish a steak. The Italians take their olive oil seriously enough that some producers guard their groves like they're state secrets, and frankly, after tasting a good Tuscan oil, you understand why.

Spanish olive oil, on the other hand, is often fruitier and slightly milder, though that's a gross simplification given that Spain produces more olive oil than any other country in the world. The stuff from Andalusia, made predominantly from Picual olives, has a grassy, slightly bitter edge that works beautifully in gazpacho or drizzled over grilled fish. Then you've got oils from Catalonia that lean sweeter and more delicate, the kind that whispers rather than shouts. If Italian olive oil is a tenor hitting a high note, Spanish oil is more of a baritone—still expressive, just less concerned with making a scene.

Greek olive oil tends to fall somewhere in the middle, with a richness that feels almost buttery. Koroneiki olives, which dominate Greek production, produce an oil that's fruity without being cloying, peppery without being aggressive. It's the sort of oil that feels at home in a Greek salad, obviously, but also works when you want something versatile enough to use across a range of dishes without overwhelming them. There's a reason the Greeks have been making olive oil for thousands of years—they've had time to figure out what works.

Then we have Portugal, which doesn't get nearly enough credit for its olive oil despite producing some genuinely excellent examples. Portuguese oils, particularly those from the Alentejo region, tend to be smooth and slightly nutty, with a mellow fruitiness that makes them ideal for dishes where you want the oil to support rather than dominate. They're less commonly found outside of Portugal, which is a shame, because they're well worth seeking out if you're the sort of person who likes to have options.

Now, all of this regional variation is fascinating if you're the type who enjoys spending Sunday afternoons comparing tasting notes (no judgement here), but it also raises a practical question: what do you actually do with all these different oils? The short answer is that extra virgin olive oil is best used when you can taste it—drizzled over finished dishes, whisked into vinaigrettes, or used as a dip for bread. Cooking with it at high heat is a waste, not because it's unsafe (that myth about olive oil's smoke point being too low is largely overstated), but because you're essentially paying for flavour compounds that get destroyed the moment things get too hot. For sautéing or frying, regular olive oil or even a neutral oil makes more sense. Save the good stuff for when it can shine.

There's also the matter of freshness, which is something most people don't think about until they've tasted truly fresh olive oil and realised what they've been missing. Olive oil doesn't improve with age like wine; it degrades. The bright, fruity flavours you're paying for start to fade within a year of pressing, and by the time you hit the two-year mark, you're left with something flat and one-dimensional. Check the harvest date on the bottle if you can find it, and if the oil has been sitting on a shelf for eighteen months, maybe consider passing. Also, store it somewhere cool and dark—not next to the stove, where it's exposed to heat and light every time you cook, slowly turning it into an expensive version of the stuff you could've bought for half the price.

And speaking of price, yes, good olive oil costs more than the generic bottles at the supermarket, but it's one of those ingredients where the difference is immediately noticeable. You don't need to remortgage your house to buy decent oil, but spending a bit more on a bottle from a reputable producer will give you something that actually tastes like olives rather than vaguely greasy nothingness. If you're buying olive oil in a clear plastic bottle for less than the cost of a decent coffee, you're probably not getting olive oil so much as a science experiment.

I realise this has turned into more of a treatise than I'd initially planned (I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book), but olive oil is one of those subjects that rewards a bit of attention. It's not just a cooking medium; it's a flavour in its own right, and once you start paying attention to where it comes from and how it's made, it's hard to go back to treating it as an afterthought. There's a reason it's been a cornerstone of Mediterranean cooking for millennia, and it's not because people in ancient Greece didn't have other options. They just knew a good thing when they tasted it.
