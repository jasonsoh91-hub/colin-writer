You can spend a small fortune assembling a pantry that looks like it belongs in a cooking magazine. Or you can stock it with a handful of things that actually get used, and build from there.

The difference between a pantry that works and one that just takes up space isn't variety—it's perspective. You don't need fifteen bottles of oil or a collection of spice blends you can't pronounce. You need the things that let you turn whatever's in your fridge into something you'd actually want to eat. Here are three that'll really make cooking a breeze.

Good olive oil

At first glance, olive oil looks like one of those things where the expensive bottle is just marketing. In practice, though, it's one of the few ingredients where spending a bit more changes everything. A proper extra virgin olive oil—fruity, slightly peppery, the kind that coats your tongue and lingers—can turn a plate of plain pasta into something you'd order at a restaurant. It's not just for drizzling over salads or finishing dishes, either. You can sauté with it, roast vegetables in it, even fry an egg in it if you're feeling indulgent. The trick is knowing when to use the good stuff and when to reach for something cheaper. For high-heat cooking, a regular olive oil works fine. But for anything where the oil itself is part of the flavour—a simple aglio e olio, a piece of grilled fish, a bowl of hummus—that's where the better bottle earns its keep. Picture this: you've just made a pot of soup, and it tastes fine but not great. A drizzle of good olive oil on top, and suddenly it's got depth, richness, a kind of warmth that wasn't there before. That has to count for something.

Soy sauce

Despite the name, soy sauce might just be the most versatile seasoning you'll ever own. It's salty, sure, but it's also deeply savoury in a way that plain salt just isn't. That umami quality—the one food writers love to go on about—is what makes soy sauce indispensable. You can use it in stir-fries, marinades, dipping sauces, even in places you wouldn't expect, like a pot of bolognese or a batch of roasted mushrooms. It adds a kind of background richness that's hard to pin down but impossible to miss. The thing about soy sauce is that not all bottles are created equal. Light soy sauce is your everyday workhorse—thinner, saltier, good for seasoning while you cook. Dark soy sauce is thicker, slightly sweet, and mostly used for colour and a deeper, more molasses-like flavour. If you're only going to keep one, go for light. And if you can find a naturally brewed soy sauce—something fermented over months rather than whipped up in a lab—it's well worth the extra ringgit or two. The difference is subtle but real, much like the difference between instant coffee and the proper stuff. You'll taste it in the finish, in the way it rounds out a dish rather than just making it saltier.

Garlic

If there's one ingredient that shows up in nearly every cuisine on the planet, it's garlic. Pungent, aromatic, oddly sweet when you cook it long enough—garlic is the backbone of flavour in ways that are easy to take for granted. Raw, it's sharp and aggressive, the kind of thing that clears your sinuses and announces itself on your breath for hours. Cooked, it mellows into something warm and almost buttery, the foundation for curries, pasta sauces, soups, and just about anything else you can think of. The beauty of garlic is that it's hard to use too much of it (unless you're the sort who finds three cloves excessive, in which case we probably wouldn't get along). You can roast whole bulbs until they're soft and spreadable, fry thinly sliced cloves until they're golden and crispy, or pound it into a paste with salt and chillies for a sambal that'll wake up any dish. It keeps for weeks if you store it somewhere cool and dry, and it's cheap enough that you can afford to be generous with it. The only real mistake you can make with garlic is burning it—once it crosses that line from golden to brown, it turns bitter and acrid, and there's no coming back from that. So keep an eye on the pan, and you'll be fine.

The next time you're tempted by some exotic ingredient you'll use once and forget about, consider stocking up on these instead. Chances are they'll see you through far more meals than whatever's currently gathering dust at the back of your cupboard.
