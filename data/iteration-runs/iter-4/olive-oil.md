You've probably stood in front of the olive oil section at the supermarket, staring at rows of bottles that all claim to be extra virgin, and wondered why some cost three times as much as others. The answer isn't as simple as you'd hope, and it's rarely printed on the label.

Olive oil should be straightforward. You press olives, you get oil. But somewhere between the grove and the grocery store, things got complicated. Not every bottle labelled "extra virgin" actually meets the standard, and not every expensive bottle is worth the price. The differences are real, though, and once you know what to look for, it's hard to go back to buying blindly.

The first thing to understand is that "extra virgin" is supposed to mean something specific. It's oil from the first cold pressing of olives, with no heat or chemicals involved, and it has to meet certain chemical benchmarks for acidity and flavour. But enforcement varies wildly depending on where you are, and plenty of bottles slip through with labels that promise more than what's inside. You'll find genuinely excellent extra virgin oils sitting next to ones that have been cut with cheaper refined oils, and the only way to tell is to taste them or trust the producer.

Italian oils tend to get the most attention, and some of them deserve it. Tuscany produces oils that are grassy and peppery, the sort that make you cough a little if you taste them straight, which is actually a good sign. Liguria, up in the north, gives you something gentler and more delicate, oils that work beautifully with fish or vegetables where you don't want to overpower the dish. But not all Italian oils are created equal, and some of the mass-market brands you'll find at Jaya Grocer or Ben's Independent Grocer are trading more on the romance of the label than the quality of what's inside.

Spanish oils, on the other hand, don't get nearly enough credit. Spain produces more olive oil than any other country, and while some of it is bulk oil destined for industrial use, the good stuff from regions like Andalusia or Catalonia can hold its own against anything from Italy. Spanish oils tend to be fruitier and slightly sweeter, less aggressive on the pepper, and they're often better value because they're not carrying the same premium for heritage branding. If you're using olive oil for cooking rather than finishing, a decent Spanish oil will do the job without making you feel like you're burning money every time you heat the pan.

Greek oils are worth seeking out if you want something with a bit more character. They're often made from Koroneiki olives, which produce oils that are intensely flavoured and slightly bitter in a way that works beautifully with tomatoes, feta, or anything involving lemon. Greek producers tend to be smaller operations, which means the quality can be more consistent, but it also means you'll pay a bit more and have to hunt around for them. They're not always sitting on the shelf next to the Bertolli.

Then there's the question of when to use what. A lot of people treat olive oil like it's too precious to cook with, saving it only for drizzling over finished dishes, but that's missing the point. A good everyday extra virgin oil can absolutely handle medium heat without breaking down or losing its flavour, and it'll make your food taste better than neutral oils like canola or sunflower. Save the expensive, single-estate stuff for raw applications—dipping bread, dressing salads, finishing soups—but don't feel guilty about cooking with a mid-range bottle that costs RM30 or RM40. That's what it's there for.

What you don't want to do is buy a massive bottle that'll sit in your cupboard for six months. Olive oil oxidises once it's opened, and after a few months, even a good oil will start tasting flat and slightly rancid. Buy smaller bottles, keep them somewhere cool and dark, and use them within a couple of months of opening. If you're the sort of person who only reaches for olive oil once in a while, you're better off with a smaller, slightly better bottle than a litre of something that'll go off before you finish it.

There's also the matter of harvest dates, which almost no one checks but probably should. Olive oil is not wine. It doesn't improve with age. The fresher, the better, and if a bottle doesn't list a harvest date or a best-before date, that's already a red flag. Some producers will give you a specific month and year, which is a good sign that they're serious about what they're doing. If all you get is a vague "produce of multiple countries," you're looking at a blend that could be months or even years old by the time it reaches you.

I haven't even touched on things like organic certification, PDO labels, or the difference between early and late harvest oils, because honestly, I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book. But the basics are enough to get you started: look for a harvest date, buy from a specific region rather than a generic blend, don't be afraid to cook with it, and for the love of everything, don't store it next to your stove where it'll get hot every time you make dinner.

If you're going to spend a bit more on one ingredient in your kitchen, olive oil is a decent place to start. It's one of those things where the difference between good and mediocre is tangible enough that you'll actually notice it, and once you've tasted a proper extra virgin oil—peppery, fruity, almost alive—it's hard to go back to the stuff that tastes like it was pressed sometime during the previous decade and has been sitting in a warehouse ever since.
