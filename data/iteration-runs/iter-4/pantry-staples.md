You can fill your pantry with every exotic spice and imported condiment if you'd like. Or you can stock three reliable staples that'll carry you through most meals without the drama. Here's the thing about cooking from the pantry: it's not about having everything, it's about having the right things. The difference between a passable meal and something you'd actually want to eat often comes down to whether you've got a few unglamorous essentials on hand. Most of us have been sold the idea that a well-stocked kitchen means rows of matching jars and ingredients we'll use once before they expire at the back of the cupboard. But the pantry staples that matter are the ones you reach for without thinking—the things that turn a handful of whatever's left in the fridge into something coherent. Here are three that'll do most of the heavy lifting.

Good cooking oil

Not olive oil, not the fancy stuff with the hand-drawn label your cousin brought back from Tuscany. Just a neutral oil with a high smoke point that won't fall apart the moment things get hot. Sunflower, grapeseed, canola—pick one and stick with it. This is the oil you use when you're actually cooking, not drizzling. The one that goes in the wok when you're stir-frying vegetables at a temperature that would make most extra virgin olive oils weep. The one that doesn't announce itself in every dish, because sometimes you want the food to taste like the food, not like you've drenched it in something with opinions. Picture this: you're halfway through frying garlic and realise you've been using your good olive oil, the one that cost as much as a decent lunch, and now it's smoking and bitter and you're standing there wondering why you do this to yourself. A proper neutral oil doesn't do that. It just works. It fries, it sautés, it makes things crispy without turning the kitchen into a smoke alarm symphony. And because it doesn't have a strong flavour, you can use it for practically anything—from searing meat to making a simple salad dressing when you can't be bothered to reach for something fancier. Keep the olive oil for finishing, for dipping bread, for moments when you want that grassy, peppery hit. But for the day-to-day business of getting food cooked and onto a plate, neutral oil is the one that quietly makes everything possible.

Soy sauce

Not just any soy sauce, mind you, but a proper naturally brewed one—the kind that's been fermented rather than chemically rushed into existence. The difference is night and day, though you wouldn't know it from the identical-looking bottles lined up at the supermarket. What you're after is something that adds depth without overwhelming, that brings out the savoury backbone of a dish rather than just making everything taste like salt and food colouring. It's the thing that makes fried rice taste like fried rice instead of just rice that happens to be fried. The thing that turns a simple stir-fry of whatever vegetables you've got lying around into something you'd willingly eat for dinner rather than suffer through out of obligation. A few splashes in a marinade, a glug in a braise, a teaspoon stirred into scrambled eggs if you're feeling adventurous—soy sauce is one of those rare ingredients that works across cuisines without making you feel like you're committing some kind of culinary crime. And here's the part that matters: it keeps. Unlike fresh herbs that wilt, or that jar of curry paste you opened once and never touched again, soy sauce sits in your cupboard (or fridge, if you're particular about these things) and waits patiently for you to need it. Which you will, more often than you think. The naturally brewed stuff costs a bit more, sure, but given how little you use per dish and how long a bottle lasts, that's not exactly a tremendous disservice to your wallet. It's an investment in not having to wonder why your cooking tastes flat when you've done everything else right.

Dried pasta

Not the fresh kind that goes off in three days, not the artisanal bronze-cut stuff that costs as much as a small meal out. Just a box or two of decent dried pasta—spaghetti, penne, whatever shape you prefer—that you can pull out when the fridge is looking sparse and inspiration is even sparser. Dried pasta is the sort of thing that makes you look more competent than you actually are, because it turns the contents of a near-empty kitchen into something resembling a proper meal. A tin of tomatoes, some garlic, a handful of whatever cheese you've got, and you're halfway to dinner. It's the baseline, the thing you fall back on when you've had a long day and can't be bothered to think too hard about what to eat. And unlike rice, which requires a bit of attention and timing, pasta is almost foolishly simple: boil water, add salt, throw it in, wait. The only way to truly mess it up is to forget about it entirely, and even then, you'd have to be impressively distracted. But here's where dried pasta earns its place on this list: it's not just a fallback. Done right—cooked in well-salted water, tossed with something that actually has flavour, finished with a bit of pasta water to bring it all together—it's legitimately good. Not "good for something I threw together in fifteen minutes" good. Just good. The kind of meal that doesn't make you feel like you've settled, even though you absolutely have. And that, for a pantry staple, is about as much as you can ask for.

The next time you're tempted to buy that jar of saffron threads or the truffle oil that'll sit unused for months, consider whether you've got these three sorted first. Chances are they'll serve you better than any number of exotic additions that sound impressive but do very little actual work. The pantry staples that matter aren't the ones that make you look like you know what you're doing—they're the ones that quietly ensure you actually do.
