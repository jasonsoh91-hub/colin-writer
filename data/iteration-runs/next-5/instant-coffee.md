**The Truth About Instant Coffee**

Instant coffee isn't bad. It's just misunderstood by people who've decided it's beneath them without ever really thinking about what it is or why it exists.

Now you might be thinking I've lost the plot entirely, what with all the specialty cafés dotting Kuala Lumpur like particularly pretentious mushrooms after rain, but hear me out. The stuff gets dismissed as a sad compromise, the thing you reach for when you can't be bothered to do coffee properly, but that's a gross simplification of what's actually a rather ingenious bit of food science that's been keeping people caffeinated since the early 1900s. You point to your freshly ground beans and your pour-over setup and I'll point to the jar of Nescafé that's probably sitting in your parents' kitchen right now, doing its job without complaint.

Take freeze-dried instant coffee, which is what you're getting if you're buying anything halfway decent. The process starts with brewed coffee that's been concentrated, then frozen at temperatures low enough to make your home freezer look like a sauna. What happens next is sublimation, which is just a fancy way of saying the ice crystals turn directly into vapour without passing through the liquid stage first. It's the same principle that makes ice cubes shrink in your freezer over time, except this is done in a vacuum chamber under controlled conditions. What's left are those familiar granules that dissolve the moment hot water hits them. The whole thing was perfected by Nestlé in 1938, and while I'm not about to write love letters to multinational corporations, you have to admit that turning liquid coffee into shelf-stable powder that reconstitutes on demand is a neat trick.

Then there's spray-dried instant coffee, which takes that same concentrated brew and fires it through a hot chamber as a fine mist. The droplets dry mid-air and fall as powder. It's faster and cheaper than freeze-drying, which is why it's the method behind most budget instant coffees. The trade-off is that the high heat can damage some of the more delicate flavour compounds, leaving you with something that tastes more like the idea of coffee than actual coffee. But even that has its place. Sometimes you just want caffeine and warmth in a cup, and there's no shame in that (hopefully).

The real problem isn't that instant coffee tastes bad. It's that we've been sold a story about what coffee should be, and instant doesn't fit the narrative. Specialty coffee culture has done wonderful things for appreciation of the bean and the craft of extraction, but it's also created a kind of snobbery that treats instant as the enemy. Walk into any kopitiam in Malaysia and you'll find people drinking instant coffee mixed with condensed milk, completely unbothered by the fact that it came from a packet. It's sweet, it's strong, and it gets the job done. Much like the city itself, nobody's pretending it's something it's not.

Of course, not all instant coffee is created equal. The freeze-dried stuff from brands like Wonda or the higher-end Nescafé Gold tastes noticeably better than the cheapest options, mostly because the process preserves more of the original coffee's character. Single-origin instant coffees have started appearing too, which is a strange sentence to write but there you go. These tend to come from specialty roasters who've realised there's a market for people who want convenience without sacrificing flavour entirely. They cost more, naturally, but if you're the sort who cares about where your beans come from, at least the option exists.

Here's what it comes down to: instant coffee is a tool. It's not trying to replace your morning ritual of grinding beans and timing your bloom phase and whatever else you do to feel alive before nine in the morning. It's there for the times when you need coffee and you need it now, or when you're travelling and the hotel kettle is your only option, or when you're at your parents' place and they're offering you a cup from the jar they've had since 2015. In those moments, instant coffee does exactly what it's supposed to do. It gives you something warm, caffeinated, and recognisable as coffee.

So maybe it's time we stopped treating it like a guilty secret. I'm not suggesting you ditch your pour-over setup or cancel your subscription to that roaster in Penang who hand-delivers beans on a vintage bicycle (shall we say, an admirably committed bit of branding). I'm just saying there's room for both. The world's complicated enough without adding coffee snobbery to the mix. If someone offers you instant, just drink it. It won't kill you, and it might even taste better than you remember.
