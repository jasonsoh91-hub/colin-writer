# How Sourdough Actually Rises

There's no magic in sourdough. There, I said it. The entire mystique around it—the talk of ancient traditions, the reverent whispers about mother starters passed down through generations, the Instagram accounts focused to scoring patterns—it's all a bit much when you consider what's actually happening. You're feeding flour and water to wild yeast and bacteria, and they're burping out gas. That's it. That's the miracle everyone's been going on about.

Now, you're probably looking at me like I've just told you Santa isn't real (he's not, by the way, but that's another article). You point to your starter jar, bubbling away on the counter, and you say, "But Colin, I've been nurturing this for weeks. I've named it. It has a personality." And I believe you. I've met people who talk to their starters. I've read forum posts where grown adults debate whether their starter prefers jazz or classical music. But the thing doing the actual work—the rising, the flavour, the structure—doesn't care about any of that. It cares about having something to eat and a place that's not too cold or too hot. The rest is just us projecting.

Take wheat flour, for instance. When you mix it with water, you're not making dough yet—you're making a buffet. The flour contains starches, and those starches are just long chains of sugar molecules waiting to be broken down. Your sourdough starter, which is really just a colony of wild yeast (mostly Saccharomyces exiguus, if you want to get specific) and lactic acid bacteria (Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis being the famous one), gets to work immediately. The bacteria produce enzymes that chop those starch chains into simple sugars. The yeast then gobbles up those sugars and, in a process called fermentation, converts them into carbon dioxide and ethanol. The carbon dioxide gets trapped in the gluten network you've been developing through all that kneading and folding, and that's what makes your dough rise. The ethanol mostly bakes off in the oven, though it does contribute a bit to the flavour. It's a symbiotic relationship that's been happening since ancient Egypt, though I doubt the Egyptians were posting about it on social media.

Then there's the question of why it takes so long. Commercial yeast—the stuff you buy in those little packets—is a single, highly efficient strain of Saccharomyces cerevisiae that's been bred to work fast. It's the thoroughbred of the yeast world. Your sourdough starter, by contrast, is more like a small village. There are multiple species of yeast and bacteria in there, all working at different speeds, all producing slightly different byproducts. The lactic acid bacteria produce lactic acid (hence the name, in a rare moment of scientific clarity), which gives sourdough its characteristic tang. Some strains also produce acetic acid, which adds a sharper, more vinegary note. This slower fermentation is why sourdough takes hours or even days to rise properly, and it's also why the flavour is so much more complex than what you get from commercial yeast. The microbes are basically having a very slow dinner party in your dough, and the longer they stay, the more interesting the conversation (flavour) becomes.

But here's where it gets oddly specific: temperature matters more than almost anything else. Your starter is most active between 24°C and 28°C. Below that, the yeast slows down. Above that, the bacteria start to dominate, and you get a more acidic loaf. This is why bakers who know what they're doing will proof their dough in the fridge overnight—the cold slows everything down, giving the bacteria time to develop flavour without producing so much acid that the dough turns into a science experiment gone wrong. It's also why your friend who insists their apartment is "the perfect temperature for sourdough" probably just has a flat that hovers around 26°C (or they're delusional, which is equally likely).

And then we have hydration, which is just baker-speak for how much water you've added relative to flour. A higher hydration dough (say, 75% water to flour) will ferment faster than a stiffer one because the microbes can move around more easily. It'll also be stickier and harder to handle, which is why every sourdough tutorial on the internet makes it look effortless while your own attempts end up resembling something that should be scraped off a pavement. The gluten network in a high-hydration dough is more extensible, meaning it can trap more gas and give you those big, irregular holes that make people think you know what you're doing. A lower hydration dough (60% or so) will rise more slowly, but it'll be easier to shape and will give you a tighter crumb. Neither is better. They're just different. Though if you're the sort of person who needs your bread to look like it belongs in a museum, you'll want to go higher.

So what does all this say about us, really? We've taken a process that's fundamentally about controlled rot—microbes breaking down starches and proteins, producing acid and gas as waste products—and turned it into a marker of sophistication. We've convinced ourselves that there's virtue in waiting three days for a loaf of bread when the supermarket will sell you one for a few ringgit. We've built entire identities around our ability to keep a jar of flour and water alive on the counter. And you know what? That's fine. Humans have always been good at taking the mundane and making it mean something. Money is just paper until we all agree it's worth something. Countries are just lines on a map until we decide they matter. A sourdough starter is just yeast and bacteria until you name it Gerald and start talking about its moods.

The lie, if you can even call it that, is that any of this is mystical or beyond understanding. It's not. It's biology. It's chemistry. It's microbes doing what microbes have been doing for millions of years, long before we figured out how to trap them in a jar and make them work for us. But we all agree to pretend there's something more to it, and in that agreement, maybe there is. Your bread rises because wild yeast produces carbon dioxide. It tastes good because bacteria produce acids during a long, slow fermentation. And it matters because you've decided it does. That has to count for something, even if it's just the satisfaction of pulling a warm loaf out of the oven and knowing, at least for a moment, that you made something with your own hands.
