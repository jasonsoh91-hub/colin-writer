You can build a sauce collection that rivals a restaurant's walk-in if you'd like. Or you can keep three bottles in your fridge that'll carry you through just about anything you're likely to cook at home.

The thing about sauces is that they're often treated as finishing touches—something you reach for at the end to rescue a bland meal. But the better move is to think of them as foundational. A good sauce doesn't just add flavour. It changes the structure of a dish entirely, turning plain rice into something you'd actually want to eat, or making yesterday's roast chicken feel like a different meal altogether. The trick is knowing which ones are worth the real estate in your fridge door. Here are three that'll really make cooking a breeze.

Oyster sauce

At first glance, oyster sauce looks like it belongs exclusively in stir-fries, and to be fair, that's where most people encounter it. But if you've ever wondered why your Chinese takeout tastes richer and more cohesive than anything you make at home, there's a good chance oyster sauce is doing a lot of the heavy lifting behind the scenes.

It's thick, slightly sweet, deeply savoury, and has this way of clinging to ingredients that makes everything taste more deliberate. You can use it to glaze vegetables, marinate meat, or add a base note to fried rice that makes the dish feel less like a collection of leftovers and more like something you planned. The sweetness isn't cloying—it's more like the kind of roundness that balances out saltiness and prevents a dish from tasting one-dimensional. Picture this: you've stir-fried some greens, maybe kai lan or choy sum, and they're sitting there looking a bit sad and watery. A tablespoon of oyster sauce tossed in at the end pulls everything together, coating each leaf in a gloss that makes the whole thing feel restaurant-quality (hopefully not literally, given how much oil some restaurants use).

The main thing to watch for is sodium content, as some brands are quite aggressive with the salt. Lee Kum Kee is the standard most people reach for, though there are vegetarian versions made with mushrooms if you're avoiding seafood or just want a slightly earthier flavour. Once opened, it'll last for months in the fridge, which means you're not racing against spoilage the way you might with fresh herbs or citrus. That has to count for something.

Gochujang

Despite the name, gochujang might just be one of the most versatile condiments you're not using enough of. It's a Korean fermented chilli paste made from red chillies, glutinous rice, fermented soybeans, and salt, and it brings heat, sweetness, umami, and a slight funk all at once. If you've only encountered it in bibimbap or tteokbokki, you're missing out on what it can do outside of Korean cooking.

The heat is there, but it's not the kind that makes you reach for water immediately. It builds slowly, and the sweetness keeps it from feeling aggressive. You can stir a spoonful into mayo for a quick burger sauce, whisk it into a vinaigrette to cut through fatty meats, or thin it out with a bit of sesame oil and rice vinegar to make a glaze for roasted vegetables. It also works surprisingly well in marinades, especially for pork or chicken, where the sugars caramelise during cooking and give you that slightly charred, sticky crust that makes grilled meat so satisfying.

The texture is thick and slightly grainy, which means it doesn't dissolve as easily as, say, sriracha, but that's part of the appeal. It clings. It coats. It doesn't just disappear into the dish. And because it's fermented, there's a depth to it that you don't get from fresh chillies or hot sauce—something almost miso-like, though not quite as assertive. Once you've got a tub of it in the fridge, you'll start finding excuses to use it, which is generally a sign you've made the right call.

Kecap manis

Kecap manis is Indonesian sweet soy sauce, and if you've never cooked with it, you're probably wondering why anyone would want their soy sauce sweetened. The answer is that it's not just soy sauce with sugar dumped in—it's thicker, more viscous, and has a molasses-like quality that makes it behave more like a glaze than a seasoning liquid.

It's the backbone of dishes like nasi goreng and mee goreng, where it adds colour, sweetness, and a kind of caramelised depth that regular soy sauce can't replicate. But it's also oddly satisfying drizzled over fried eggs, tossed with roasted Brussels sprouts, or used as a marinade for grilled chicken. The sweetness is pronounced, but it's balanced by the saltiness and umami of the soy, so it doesn't taste like dessert. It just tastes like something that knows how to hold a dish together.

One thing worth noting: because it's so thick, it doesn't disperse evenly if you're adding it to a sauce or marinade without thinning it out first. A splash of water or a bit of lime juice usually does the trick. And if you're in a pinch and don't have it on hand, you can approximate it by mixing regular soy sauce with palm sugar or brown sugar, though the flavour won't be quite as complex. ABC is the brand you'll see most often, and it's cheap enough that there's no reason not to keep a bottle around.

The next time you're tempted by some obscure artisanal condiment with a label that looks like it was designed by someone who owns a lot of linen, consider whether you'd actually use it more than once. Chances are these three will do more for your cooking than a dozen half-used jars slowly crystallising in the back of your fridge.
