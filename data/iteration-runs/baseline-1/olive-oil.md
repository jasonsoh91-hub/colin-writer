# All About Olive Oil

Is olive oil really worth the fuss, or have we all been collectively convinced to spend three times as much on what is essentially just pressed olives? The answer, frustratingly, is both. There's a lot of olive oil out there that doesn't deserve your money, and then there's the stuff that makes you understand why people have been pressing olives for thousands of years and still haven't got bored of it.

The Mediterranean has been at this for somewhere around 6,000 years, which means olive oil predates most of the foods we consider ancient by a comfortable margin. It started in the eastern Mediterranean—modern-day Syria, Lebanon, and that general vicinity—before spreading west to Greece, Italy, and Spain, where it became so integral to cooking that you'd be hard-pressed to find a dish that doesn't involve it in some capacity. The Greeks used it for everything from cooking to lighting lamps to rubbing on their bodies before wrestling, which seems like a tremendous waste of good oil, but I suppose they had their reasons.

Extra virgin olive oil is the one you've probably heard the most about, and it's extra virgin because it comes from the first cold pressing of the olives, with no heat or chemicals involved. It has to meet strict acidity standards—less than 0.8%—and it's the only grade that actually tastes like olives. You'll find fruity notes, peppery finishes, grassy undertones, and all manner of other descriptors that sound like wine tasting but are, in this case, entirely justified. This is the oil you want for finishing dishes, drizzling over salads, or dipping bread into while you wait for your pasta to arrive. Cooking with it isn't wrong, exactly, but it's a bit like using vintage champagne to make sangria.

Virgin olive oil is the slightly less prestigious sibling, still from the first pressing but with a higher acidity level—up to 2%—and a less refined flavour profile. It's perfectly good for cooking, especially if you're sautéing vegetables or making a tomato-based sauce where the oil's flavour will meld into the background rather than take centre stage. You won't find it as easily in Malaysian supermarkets, which tend to stock either extra virgin or the more processed varieties, but it exists in the middle ground for those who want some olive character without paying extra virgin prices.

Pure olive oil, despite the name, is a blend of refined olive oil and a small percentage of virgin olive oil to give it some semblance of flavour. The refining process strips out most of the natural taste and aroma, leaving you with something neutral enough for high-heat cooking but not particularly interesting to eat on its own. This is what you reach for when you're frying chicken or making something that requires a neutral oil with a higher smoke point than extra virgin can handle. It's functional, which is not an insult, just a statement of purpose.

Light olive oil has nothing to do with calories or fat content, which is a common misconception that the marketing teams behind these bottles are perfectly happy to let slide. It's called light because the flavour and colour are lighter, the result of even more refining. It has a higher smoke point than extra virgin—around 240°C compared to extra virgin's 190°C—which makes it useful for deep frying or other high-heat applications where you don't want any olive flavour coming through. If you're making tempura or frying doughnuts, this is a reasonable choice, though you could just as easily use vegetable oil and save yourself a few ringgit.

The Spanish make some of the best olive oil in the world, particularly from Andalusia, where the climate and terrain create ideal conditions for olives. Their oils tend to be fruity and slightly bitter, with a peppery kick that hits the back of your throat if you're tasting it straight. The Italians, meanwhile, produce oils that range from delicate and buttery in Liguria to robust and grassy in Tuscany, and the Greeks favour a more aggressive, almost herbal style that pairs beautifully with feta and tomatoes. You'll find all of these on the shelves at Jaya Grocer or Ben's Independent Grocer, often sitting next to each other with wildly different price tags that reflect not just quality but also the region's reputation and the producer's scale.

There's a lot I haven't covered here—the difference between early and late harvest, the role of terroir, the ongoing problem of fraudulent labelling in the olive oil industry, the fact that some of the most expensive bottles are genuinely worth it while others are just expensive—but I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book if I wanted to get into all that. What matters is knowing that not all olive oil is the same, and that the bottle you pick should match what you're planning to do with it.

If you've been using the same generic olive oil for everything from salad dressings to stir-fries, it might be worth splitting your olive oil budget into two bottles: one good extra virgin for finishing and one pure or light for cooking. Your food won't suddenly transform into something Michelin-starred, but you'll start noticing the difference in how things taste when the oil is doing what it's supposed to do rather than just being there because the recipe said so.
