For a lot of us, building a pantry feels like an arms race we're destined to lose. You start with good intentions—salt, pepper, maybe some pasta—and before you know it, you've got three kinds of vinegar you've never opened, a jar of harissa that's fossilised at the back of the shelf, and a spice blend from that one recipe you made two years ago. It's exhausting, and most of it just sits there, quietly judging you every time you order takeout.

The truth is, you don't need much. A handful of things that pull their weight, that show up when you need them, and that don't require a flowchart to figure out when to use—that's the real pantry.

Soy sauce is one of those things that does far more than you'd think for something that costs less than a plate of chicken rice. It's not just for stir-fries or dipping your gyoza. A splash of it deepens the flavour of pretty much anything savoury—pasta sauces, stews, even scrambled eggs if you're feeling adventurous. The salt brings things forward, the umami fills in the gaps, and suddenly your boring weeknight dinner tastes like you put in more effort than you actually did. Keep a bottle of light soy sauce around, and if you want to get fancy, grab a bottle of dark soy for when you need colour and a touch of sweetness. Between the two, you've got most situations covered.

Chilli oil sits in that rare category of condiments that make you wonder how you ever lived without it. Not the kind that's just oil with some heat—get the good stuff, the kind with crunchy bits of garlic and shallot suspended in it, the kind that smells like someone's been frying things all day in the best possible way. It's what turns plain noodles into something you'd willingly eat three times a week. It's what makes dumplings worth the effort. Drizzle it over rice, stir it into soups, spoon it onto fried eggs, or just eat it straight off a piece of toast if no one's watching (they shouldn't be). The trick is to not overthink it—if something tastes a bit flat, chilli oil will sort it out.

Garlic doesn't need much of an introduction, but it's worth saying that most people don't use enough of it. A clove or two is fine if you're being polite, but if you want actual flavour, you're looking at four, five, maybe six cloves depending on what you're making. It's the backbone of almost every cuisine that's worth paying attention to, and it behaves completely differently depending on how you treat it. Sliced thin and fried until golden, it turns sweet and crispy. Smashed and sautéed, it mellows out and becomes the base for everything from aglio e olio to a proper sambal. Roasted whole, it goes soft and spreadable, almost caramelised. You can't really go wrong with garlic unless you're burning it, and even then, some of us have done that and kept going (hopefully not literally).

I'm not going to tell you that these three things will solve every cooking problem you've ever had, or that you'll suddenly become the kind of person who meal-preps on Sundays and has their spice drawer alphabetised. But they'll get you through most nights when you're too tired to think and still want to eat something that doesn't come in a plastic container.

The other day, I made rice, fried an egg, and dumped chilli oil over the whole thing. That was dinner. It was good. That has to count for something.
