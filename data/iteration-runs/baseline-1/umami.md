# How Umami Actually Works: The Science Of The Fifth Taste

Umami isn't real. Or at least, it wasn't real until someone decided it should be. There, I said it.

You're probably looking at me like I've lost my mind. Fair enough. You point to the dashi, the Parmesan, the aged beef, the tomato paste you've been using your whole life—all those things that make food taste fuller, rounder, more satisfying. You're telling me that's not umami? But here's the thing: umami only became "real" in 1908 when a Japanese chemist gave it a name. Before that, it was just good cooking. The taste existed, obviously, but so did a hundred other tastes we've since decided don't matter enough to name. We built the category, then convinced ourselves we'd discovered something fundamental about reality (which, to be fair, is how most of science works).

Monosodium glutamate is the ur-example here. It's the crystallised form of the umami taste, isolated by Kikunae Ikeda from a pot of kombu dashi in Tokyo. He called it *umami*, meaning "pleasant savoury taste", patented the production process, and within two decades MSG was everywhere. The thing is, glutamate appears naturally in tomatoes, aged cheese, cured meat, mushrooms, and basically anything that ferments or ages long enough. We didn't invent a new taste—we just figured out how to bottle something that was already in the soy sauce. But give it a name, package it in a shaker, and suddenly it's a scandal waiting to happen.

Tomatoes are the Western world's umami gateway drug, though nobody talks about it that way. A ripe tomato contains about 250 milligrams of glutamate per 100 grams, which is why tomato paste, sun-dried tomatoes, and ketchup all hit that savoury-sweet spot harder than the fresh fruit ever could. Concentration is everything. The Italians understood this instinctively—*sugo di pomodoro* isn't just tomato sauce, it's hours of simmering that breaks down cell walls and concentrates glutamate until the sauce clings to pasta with a kind of umami-rich insistence. And yet, if you'd asked an Italian grandmother in 1900 what umami was, she'd have looked at you like you'd grown a second head. She was just making lunch.

Parmesan cheese, or Parmigiano-Reggiano if we're being specific, clocks in at around 1,200 milligrams of free glutamate per 100 grams after 24 months of aging. That's five times the glutamate content of tomatoes, which explains why a handful of grated Parmesan can rescue an otherwise bland risotto or why people eat it in chunks with a drizzle of balsamic like it's dessert. The aging process breaks down milk proteins into free amino acids, glutamate chief among them. The longer it ages, the more crystals form—those crunchy bits you get in a well-aged wedge. Those are pure umami, crystallised into something you can crunch between your teeth. It's not magic, it's biochemistry, but we had to wait until the 20th century to admit that.

Anchovies are umami concentrate disguised as a small, oily fish. They're also the secret ingredient in about half the dishes you've ever loved and never knew contained anchovies. Caesar dressing, puttanesca, bagna càuda, Worcestershire sauce—anchovies are in all of them, breaking down into pure savoury depth. A single anchovy fillet contains around 630 milligrams of glutamate per 100 grams, and because they're usually used in small amounts, you never taste "fish"—you just taste more. It's the same principle as MSG, except culturally acceptable. Add a minced anchovy to a tomato sauce and nobody calls it cheating. Add a pinch of MSG and suddenly you're a fraud (which says more about us than it does about the anchovy).

Soy sauce is fermented soybeans, wheat, salt, and time—usually at least six months, sometimes years. The fermentation process, driven by *Aspergillus* mould and lactic acid bacteria, breaks down proteins into amino acids, glutamate included. A tablespoon of soy sauce contains around 1,000 milligrams of glutamate, which is why a few drops can transform fried rice from bland to essential. The Japanese have been using it since the 17th century, the Chinese longer, and yet the West didn't have a proper word for what it did to food until Ikeda coined *umami*. We just called it "savoury" and left it at that, as if savoury was a satisfying explanation for why some foods make you want to lick the plate.

The thing is, umami only works because we all agreed it works. Taste is partly biology—glutamate binds to specific receptors on your tongue, triggering a signal your brain interprets as "savoury"—but it's also culture. The Japanese named it, the food industry marketed it, and now we treat it like the fifth taste, as fundamental as sweet or salty. But taste categories aren't natural laws. They're linguistic conveniences, ways of organising sensation into something we can talk about. There are cultures that recognise six tastes, or seven, or more. The Ayurvedic tradition lists six: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, pungent, and astringent. Where's umami in that framework? Nowhere, because they didn't need it. We build the boxes, then pretend the boxes were always there.

And yet, I'll keep using the word. I'll keep talking about umami-rich ingredients and how to layer them in a dish, because the fiction is useful. It gives us a shared language, a way to describe why Parmesan and tomatoes and anchovies all do the same thing to a sauce. It's not real, but it's real enough. We've all agreed to pretend, and that's fine. That's how meaning works.
