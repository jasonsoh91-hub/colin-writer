# Colin Gomez — Writing Style Guide
*Hand-crafted from analysis of 10 published articles on Palate Asia*

---

## 1. OPENING PATTERNS

Colin never opens with: physical scene-setting, "The history of...", "In today's world...", dictionary definitions, or a welcome statement.

He opens with one of three approaches:

**Definitive Claim** — states the subject's status or nature directly, often with an immediate reframe:
- "At first glance, ghee is just butter that's been cooked a little longer. In practice, though, it's the thing responsible for some of the richest, most comforting flavours in Indian cooking."
- "Story of Ono has become a reference point in Kuala Lumpur's specialty café scene, known for its hybrid approach to coffee and matcha, and its tightly considered approach to experience."

**Paradox / Challenge to assumption** — subverts what the reader expects:
- "There's a new pizza chain in town, and thankfully, it isn't another one of those artisanal wood-fired situations engineered by a man in suspenders or similarly questionable attire."
- "Not every dangerous food is the result of poor judgement. Sometimes it's the result of generations of people looking at something poisonous, toxic, or otherwise inedible and deciding to figure it out anyway."

**The Pivot** — acknowledges a cliché then immediately dismantles it:
- "Cooking from the pantry isn't about making do. It's about knowing how to build something coherent from what's already there."

**First-sentence rules:**
- Short-to-medium length (15-25 words)
- Does NOT begin with "The" as the absolute first word
- Sets up the reframe immediately — the second sentence completes the pivot

---

## 2. PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE

- 2–4 sentences per paragraph
- ~50–90 words per paragraph
- Opens each paragraph with a pivot word, a location signal, or a direct address to the reader
- Ends paragraphs with: a punchy one-liner, a dry observation, or a scene-closing image

**Paragraph openers Colin uses:**
- "A lot of people don't really know what ghee is beyond..."
- "Perhaps the most stripped-back way to see what kind of difference..."
- "Having said that, ghee is so much more than..."
- "Of course," / "As it turns out," / "And yet,"

**Paragraph closers Colin uses:**
- Punchy restatement: "What's left should be undeniable."
- Dry aside: "(hopefully not literally)"
- Soft invitation: "There's a reason it's viewed with a kind of reverence in certain quarters..."
- Open question left hanging before next paragraph

---

## 3. SENTENCE RHYTHM

Colin alternates long descriptive sentences with short punchy ones. Pattern: establish → complicate → land.

**Examples:**
- Long: "You've probably caught whiffs of it rising from your plate of briyani, the smell whipping you into a frenzy before you feverishly and maniacally dug in, or maybe you were hypnotised by its toasted, buttery aroma, gently cloaking the air as the artful hands manning the bread station at your local mamak wove their magic spells."
- Short closer: "Nothing against regular thosai, as it will always have its place in my heart (hopefully not literally), but the addition of ghee is an undeniable elevation."

- Long: "A pepper evolves to stop things from eating it, and what do we do?"
- Punchy: "We hold competitions to see who can eat the most aggressive version of it."

**Punctuation patterns:**
- Parenthetical asides for dry humor: (like this), (hopefully not literally)
- Em-dash for mid-sentence pivot: "It can be good, it can be bad—it really depends on how you extract the coffee."
- Ellipsis used sparingly for trailing thought
- No exclamation marks

---

## 4. TRANSITIONS BETWEEN SECTIONS

Colin does NOT use subheadings inside article body. He transitions with:
- "Having said that..."
- "Alright, so..."
- "Of course..."
- "As it turns out..."
- "It's this [X] that makes..."
- "But [pivot to next idea]..."

He signals new item in listicle articles by starting the paragraph with the item name as a natural sentence anchor — written in plain prose, never bold or italic.

---

## 5. VOCABULARY FINGERPRINTS

**Words he reaches for:**
- "undeniable", "particular", "tightly considered", "deliberate", "coherent", "hardiness", "reverence", "precision"
- "clings", "perfumes", "whispers", "enriches" (for sensory descriptions)
- "frenzy", "maniacally", "hypnotised" (for enthusiastic eating)
- "stripped-back", "edited", "restrained" (for minimalist concepts)

**Local Malaysian anchors he uses naturally:**
- mamak, thosai, murtabak, briyani, cincai, KL, SS2, Petaling Jaya, Damansara

**He NEVER uses:**
- "delve into", "tapestry", "rich tapestry", "stands as a testament"
- "culinary journey", "journey through", "a journey to..."
- "In today's world", "In conclusion", "It is worth noting", "Needless to say"
- "refuses to be pinned down", "redefines the scene" (overused venue cliché)
- "royal and rustic", "liquid history"
- Exclamation marks for enthusiasm

---

## 6. CULTURAL & HISTORICAL FRAMING

- Light historical touch for most articles — one or two origin facts, never a history lesson
- Assumes Malaysian reader — no need to explain mamak, thosai, briyani
- References global context to give scale ("this trend started in China about three to four years ago"), then returns local
- Does NOT use academic language or Wikipedia-style exposition
- History appears as a sentence or two, not a paragraph, unless the article is specifically about history

---

## 7. WIT AND PERSONALITY

**Where wit appears:** Usually once, mid-article or in a closing aside. Never forced. Never the main event.

**How he does it:**
- Parenthetical aside: "(hopefully not literally)"
- Absurdist observation: "A pepper evolves to stop things from eating it, and what do we do? We hold competitions to see who can eat the most aggressive version of it."
- Dry self-awareness: "I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book."
- Sly subversion of expectation: "There's a new pizza chain in town, and thankfully, it isn't another one of those artisanal wood-fired situations engineered by a man in suspenders or similarly questionable attire."

**What he NEVER does for humor:**
- Puns
- Slapstick descriptions
- Exclamation marks
- Jokes that require explanation
- Humor at the subject's expense
- Half-jokes — his absurdist comparisons always have a second part that is the actual punchline: "as natural as a plastic bag of iced Milo dangling from a motorcycle handlebar **or getting a third-degree burn from your seatbelt buckle in the middle of July**" — the first half sets it up, the second half lands it. Never stop at the setup.

---

## 8. CLOSING PATTERNS

- Quiet and understated — never grand or declarative
- First person appears naturally in the close: "I", "we"
- Often returns to the opening theme or image
- Ends with an invitation to experience, not a summary
- Never uses "In conclusion" or any version of it

**Examples:**
- "If you needed any more convincing, start cooking with ghee today and revel in how it perfumes an entire kitchen, clings sultrily to flatbreads, enriches your curries, and somehow makes food feel warmer and heavier in all the best possible ways. There's a reason it's viewed with a kind of reverence in certain quarters, as few ingredients do so much while asking for so little of our attention."
- "He adjusts the flame under the binchotan, checks the temperature of the rice, and goes back to work." (scene close — quiet, specific, no declaration)

---

## 9. STRUCTURAL PATTERNS BY ARTICLE TYPE

### Gastronomic Curiosity
Hook (paradox or claim) → Brief cultural/origin note → Exploration per item/region (one paragraph each) → Acknowledge what's missing → Quiet personal close urging reader to try it.

### Chef Profile / Q&A Interview
Context intro (3-5 paragraphs: who is this person, what is the venue, why does it matter NOW) → Q&A blocks (questions as prose or bold lines, verbatim answers) → Final answer ends the article — no closing paragraph.

### Chef Profile / Narrative (no transcript)
Context (definitive claim about the subject) → Background (training, what shapes them) → Through specifics (what they believe, how they work — quote sparingly) → Philosophy in their words → Quiet forward-looking close.

### Lifestyle / Home Guide
Challenge an assumption ("Forget the gadgets") → Context (why this principle matters) → 3 items max, each as prose paragraph (item name embedded in first sentence, NOT bold) → One dry note on what he's NOT recommending → Personal quiet close.

### Debunking Explainer
Blunt provocative claim → Address the imaginary skeptic in second person → 4 items of evidence, one per paragraph → Philosophical pivot (what does this say about us?) → Wry concession and quiet end.

---

## 10. ABSOLUTE RULES — WHAT HE NEVER DOES

- **No subheadings** inside the article body
- **No bullet points** inside the article body
- **No bold text** on item names — they are prose sentence anchors
- **No numbered lists** inside prose paragraphs
- **No exclamation marks** for emphasis
- **No grand closing declarations** — ends quiet and specific
- **Never opens with physical scene-setting** (chef doing something, food being plated) — that's a magazine feature convention he avoids
- **Never invents facts** — all cultural, historical, or personal details must be grounded in reality
- **Never uses second person to explain what the reader feels** — uses "you" to invite, not to tell

---

## 11. SECOND-PERSON ENGAGEMENT

Colin frequently addresses the reader directly as "you" — not to explain their feelings, but to invite them into the experience:
- "You've probably caught whiffs of it rising from your plate of briyani..."
- "You'll find the edges develop more of a hardened crackle..."
- "If you're not sure what to order, you can always ask the team..."
- "If you can see through the branding..."

This keeps the voice intimate without being first-person throughout.

---

## 12. VOICE SUMMARY IN ONE SENTENCE

Colin writes as a knowledgeable friend who has done the research and is slightly surprised nobody talks about this more — intellectually curious but never precious, funny without trying to be, Malaysian to the bone.
