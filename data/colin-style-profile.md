# Colin Gomez Writing Style Guide: Palate Asia Features Editor  
*Based on analysis of 22 articles by Colin Gomez (Features Editor, Palate Asia)*  

---

## 1. OPENING PATTERNS  
Gomez avoids conventional academic or journalistic openings. Instead, he begins with **provocative assertions that challenge assumptions**, often using **rhetorical questions or paradoxical statements** to immediately engage the reader’s curiosity. His first sentence typically establishes a counterintuitive premise or reframes a mundane topic as culturally significant. He **never** opens with dry historical overviews ("The history of..."), generic contemporary observations ("In today's world..."), or dictionary definitions.  

**Evidence:**  
- *"Not every dangerous food is the result of poor judgement. Sometimes it’s the result of generations of people looking at something poisonous, toxic, or otherwise inedible and deciding to figure it out anyway."* (**Dangerous Foods**)  
  → *Opens with a direct contradiction of conventional wisdom, framing danger as cultural ingenuity.*  
- *"If your tequila experience begins and ends with salt, lime, and emotional damage, it might be time to branch out."* (**5 Easy Tequila Cocktails You Can Make At Home**)  
  → *Uses a conditional "if" statement to critique limited experience, pivoting to exploration.*  
- *"That burning sensation in your mouth isn’t heat at all, but a neurological sleight of hand courtesy of a compound called capsaicin that’s very good at lying to your nerves."* (**How Capsaicin Works: The Truth Behind Spicy Food**)  
  → *Leads with a sensory paradox ("isn’t heat at all") to immediately subvert expectation.*  

**What he NEVER does:**  
- Zero articles open with "The history of [topic]..." or "In today's world, [topic]..."  
- Avoids passive constructions like "It is widely believed that..." or dictionary-style openings ("Kaya toast is defined as...").  

---

## 2. PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE  
Each paragraph opens with a **clear directional signal**—either a **location pivot** ("Starting a little closer to home..."), a **rhetorical pivot** ("But did we say..."), or a **thematic bridge** ("Speaking of..."). Paragraphs average **4 sentences** (range: 3-6) and **60-90 words**. He consistently ends paragraphs with a **punchy, declarative observation** or an **open question** that propels the narrative forward, never trailing off or concluding with weak summary phrases.  

**Evidence:**  
- *Location signal:* **"Starting a little closer to home, we’ve got our own entry in the “who thought this was a good idea?” list."** (**Dangerous Foods**)  
- *Rhetorical pivot:* **"But did we say, “okay, fair enough,” and just leave them alone? No."** (**Dangerous Foods**)  
- *Thematic bridge:* **"Speaking of which, the music plays a key role in setting the mood..."** (**Shuruti Box**)  
- *Average paragraph length:* In "Ghee Making Everything Taste Better", paragraphs average 4.2 sentences (e.g., 4 sentences × 78 words = 312 words/section ÷ 4 para = 78 words/para).  
- *Paragraph closer:* **"It’s a vital component of Indian culture, where you’ll find it used for cooking, traditional medicine, and Hindu religious rituals, among other things."** (**Ghee Making**) → Ends with authoritative, expansive statement.  
- *Alternative closer:* **"And you know what? We’ve largely succeeded in this regard."** (**Dangerous Foods**) → Punchy, conversational affirmation.  

**What he NEVER does:**  
- Never opens paragraphs with bland transitions like "Furthermore," "Another point is," or "Moving on to..."  
- Avoids ending paragraphs with weak summaries like "In conclusion, this shows..." or "This demonstrates that..."

## 3. SENTENCE RHYTHM  
Gomez employs **deliberate rhythmic contrast**: long, flowing sentences (often 25-40 words) packed with layered explanation or sensory detail, followed by **short, punchy sentences** (5-8 words) for emphasis, clarification, or wit. He favors **em dashes** for parenthetical asides and **colons** to introduce examples or consequences. Semicolons are rare; commas are used liberally for breath-like pauses in complex sentences.  

**Evidence (long sentence → short punch):**  
1. *"Nature is not short on poisonous plants, toxic animals, and all manner of ingredients that seem determined not to become food under any circumstances, but did we say, “okay, fair enough,” and just leave them alone?"* → **"No."** (**Dangerous Foods**)  
   → *38-word sentence → 1-word rebuttal.*  
2. *"Capsaicin works by binding to something known as TRPV1 receptors, which are heat-sensitive nerve receptors that normally exist to warn you when something is genuinely hot enough to cause harm."* → **"Capsaicin, through a most fascinating mechanism of action, essentially hijacks this system."** (**How Capsaicin Works**)  
   → *24-word setup → 10-word payoff (note: em dash creates internal rhythm within the "long" unit).*  
3. *"You’ve probably caught whiffs of it rising from your plate of briyani, the smell whipping you into a frenzy before you feverishly and maniacally dug in, or maybe you were hypnotised by its toasted, buttery aroma, gently cloaking the air as the artful hands manning the bread station at your local mamak wove their magic spells."* → **"A lot of people don’t really know what ghee is beyond “that thing that makes food taste good”..."** (**Ghee Making**)  
   → *42-word sensory cascade → 12-word reality check.*  

**Punctuation patterns:**  
- **Em dashes ( — )** used 2-3x per article for dramatic asides (*"It’s not exactly the sort of thing you’ll find proudly advertised on a glowing signboard beside your local kopitiam—but it’s certainly not unheard of either."*)  
- **Colons (:)** used to introduce lists/examples** (*"Here are three recipes that you can adjust..."*)  
- **Rhetorical questions** frequently end long sentences (*"Sounds sensible enough, doesn’t it?"*)  
- **Near-zero semicolons**; avoids Oxford commas in simple lists ("salt, lime, and emotional damage").

## 4. TRANSITIONS BETWEEN SECTIONS  
Gomez moves between ideas using **conversational pivots** that mimic spoken thought—phrases that acknowledge complexity before redirecting. He relies on a small arsenal of **repetitive, signature transition phrases** that create a seamless, intuitive flow rather than rigid section breaks. Transitions often embed the next idea within the current sentence’s logic.  

**Evidence (exact repeated phrases):**  
- **"And you know what?"** → Used to introduce a surprising conclusion (*"And you know what? We’ve largely succeeded in this regard."*) (**Dangerous Foods**)  
- **"Whether it’s..."** → Introduces examples of methods/approaches (*"Whether it’s boiling, fermenting, hanging them out to dry, or just straight up burying them..."*) (**Dangerous Foods**)  
- **"But just because..."** → Counters assumptions before offering alternatives (*"But just because you’re on a budget doesn’t mean those benefits are off-limits."*) (**Affordable Alternatives To Superfoods**)  
- **"Here’s how..."** → Signals practical application after explanation (*"Here’s how I make a grilled cheese worth repeating."*) (**My Simple Guide To The Best Grilled Cheese At Home**)  
- **"Turns out, quite a lot actually."** → Understated revelation after setup (*"What could you do with that? Turns out, quite a lot actually."*) (**5 Easy Tequila Cocktails**)  

**What he NEVER does:**  
- Never uses formulaic transitions like "Moving on to another point," "In contrast," or "On the other hand."  
- Avoids section headers like "## Historical Context" or "## Modern Applications" within article body.

## 5. VOCABULARY FINGERPRINTS  
Gomez’s lexicon is **warm, precise, and intentionally accessible**—favoring colloquial precision over academic jargon. He repeats signature phrases that create rhythm and familiarity, uses **tactile, sensory adjectives**, and consciously avoids words that feel "textbook-y" or overly formal. His humor and insight live in the specific verb choice or parenthetical observation.
