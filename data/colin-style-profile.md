**Colin Gomez – Writing‑Style Guide**  
*(derived from the two available Gomez pieces: “Barilla Pasta Championship Asia 2025 Crowns Chef Aaron Gulinao As Grand Champion” and “From Coolie Drink To Cool Again: The Toddy Revival”)*  

---  

## 1. Opening Hook Patterns  
| Observation | Example | How to Replicate |
|-------------|---------|------------------|
| **Bold, declarative statement** that instantly frames the subject | “Toddy is Malaysia’s other palm wine.” | Open with a single‑sentence fact that positions the topic in a larger cultural or categorical context. |
| **Contrast‑driven lead** that pits the ordinary against the extraordinary | “From eight nations came eight champions, **but** it was Chef Aaron Joseph Gulinao whose Filipino‑inspired pasta captured the judges’ attention …” | Begin with a plural/group scenario, then insert a **but** (or **however**) that singles out the story’s focal point. |
| **Personal invitation** (often follows the hook) | “But I’m here to talk about a different kind of palm wine today …” | After the factual/contrast hook, shift to first‑person voice to signal that the piece will be a guided exploration, not just a report. |

**Rule of thumb:** *Hook → Contrast or Personal pivot → Brief context.*  

---  

## 2. Sentence Rhythm & Length Variety  
- **Mix of short, punchy sentences** (5‑8 words) and **longer, layered sentences** (20‑35 words).  
- Short sentences often deliver a **core fact** or **emotional punch**.  
- Longer sentences weave **background, qualification, and a hint of personality** (e.g., clauses set off by commas, em‑dashes, or parentheses).  

**Typical pattern:**  

1. **Short fact** – establishes the headline idea.  
2. **Medium sentence** – adds nuance or a counterpoint.  
3. **Long sentence** – supplies history, description, or a personal aside.  
4. **Return to short** – for emphasis or transition.  

*Example from the Toddy piece:*  

> Toddy is Malaysia’s other palm wine. **(short)**  
> A drink once maligned, but now quietly making its way back into the spotlight. **(medium‑long, contrast)**  
> When you think of locally derived alcoholic beverages, it’s likely tuak that comes to mind. **(medium, rhetorical question‑style)**  
> But I’m here to talk about a different kind of palm wine today, one that doesn’t get as much love, owing perhaps to its coloured past. **(long, personal pivot)**

## 3. Structural Pattern (How Each Piece Moves)  

| Section | Purpose | Typical Content |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **1. Hook** | Grab attention, set stakes | Declarative fact or contrast + personal invitation |
| **2. Context / Background** | Ground the topic in culture/history | Timeline (“first began in the late 19th century”), ethnic references, cultural analogues (e.g., tuak vs. toddy) |
| **3. Personal Angle / Voice** | Show why the writer cares | First‑person statements (“I’m here to talk about…”, “I reckon…”) |
| **4. Detail / Development** | Deliver the meat – event description, tasting notes, quotes, process description | Specifics of the event, dish, drink, or practice; sensory adjectives; occasional quotes from protagonists |
| **5. Reflection / Forward‑look** | Close with a takeaway or implication | Outlook (“quietly making its way back into the spotlight”), a call to appreciate, or a nod to future relevance |
| **6. Closing Sentence** | Leave a resonant image or thought | Often a concise, evocative line that echoes the opening theme (e.g., a nod to “glow‑up” or “renaissance”) |
