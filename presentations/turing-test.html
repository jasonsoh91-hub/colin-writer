You've probably drizzled olive oil over salad without giving it much thought. But if you've ever stood in front of a supermarket shelf staring at bottles ranging from RM15 to RM150, you'll know there's more going on here than meets the eye.

Olive oil has been around for thousands of years, which sounds like the sort of thing that gets said about every ingredient with any sort of pedigree, but in this case it's worth noting because the basic process hasn't changed all that much. You crush olives, you press them, you collect what comes out. The Phoenicians were doing it, the Romans were doing it, and now someone in Tuscany is doing it while charging you three times as much because the label has a drawing of a villa on it. What has changed, though, is how we talk about it, and more importantly, how we've managed to complicate what should be a relatively straightforward purchase.

The first thing to understand is that "extra virgin" isn't just marketing speak designed to make you feel better about spending more money. It's a technical designation that means the oil was extracted using mechanical methods only—no heat, no chemicals—and it has to meet specific standards for acidity and flavour. If an olive oil is labelled extra virgin, it should taste like olives, which sounds obvious until you realise how many bottles out there taste like nothing at all or, worse, like the inside of a cardboard box that's been left out in the rain. You'll find extra virgin olive oil from Spain, Italy, Greece, and increasingly from places like California and Australia, each with their own character shaped by climate, soil, and the particular variety of olive being pressed. Spanish oils, often made from Picual or Arbequina olives, tend to be robust and slightly peppery, the sort of thing that makes itself known when you drizzle it over tomatoes. Italian oils can range from delicate and buttery in the north to grassy and assertive in the south, and Greek oils—often from Koroneiki olives—bring a fruity brightness that works beautifully with grilled fish or a simple horiatiki.

Then there's "virgin" olive oil, which is produced the same way as extra virgin but doesn't quite meet the same standards for flavour or acidity. It's still mechanically extracted, still free of chemical processing, but it might taste a little flatter or have a slight defect that keeps it from the top tier. You're less likely to see it on shelves here, as most producers either achieve extra virgin status or drop down to the next category entirely. That next category is simply labelled "olive oil" or sometimes "pure olive oil," which is a polite way of saying it's been refined. This is oil that started out with too many flaws to be sold as virgin, so it gets treated with heat and chemicals to strip out the off-flavours, then blended with a bit of virgin oil to give it back some character. It's cheaper, it's more neutral, and it's what you'd reach for if you're frying something and don't want to spend extra virgin prices on oil that's just going to break down under high heat anyway.

There's also "light" or "extra light" olive oil, which has nothing to do with calories and everything to do with flavour. It's been refined even further to remove most of the olive taste, leaving you with something that behaves more like vegetable oil. If you're baking a cake and you don't want any interference from assertive olive notes, this is the one. But if you're buying olive oil because you actually want it to taste like olives, this isn't going to do much for you.

What complicates all of this is that olive oil, unlike wine, doesn't improve with age. In fact, it gets worse. The moment olives are pressed, the oil begins a slow decline, losing the bright, peppery notes that make good olive oil worth seeking out in the first place. That's why harvest dates matter more than you might think, and why those dusty bottles that have been sitting on a shelf for two years are probably past their best (hopefully not literally, though with olive oil you really can't be too sure). If you can find a bottle with a harvest date rather than just a best-before date, you're already ahead. Freshness is everything, which is why some of the best oils come in dark glass or tins—they're protecting the oil from light, which is one of its main enemies along with heat and oxygen.

And yet, despite all this, there's an entire world of fraudulent olive oil out there, which sounds dramatic until you realise just how common it is. You'll find bottles labelled "extra virgin" that have been cut with cheaper oils, or oils labelled "Product of Italy" that were actually produced elsewhere and just bottled in Italy, or oils that meet the legal definition of extra virgin but taste like something you'd use to lubricate a bicycle chain. It's not that all inexpensive olive oil is bad, but it does mean that price can be a useful indicator, even if it's not a guarantee. If you're paying less than RM30 for a litre of extra virgin olive oil, you should at least ask yourself why.

I haven't even touched on things like cold-pressed versus first-pressed, or the difference between PDO and PGI designations, or what it means when a bottle says "single estate" versus "blend." If I kept going, I'd need to slap a cover on this and call it a book. The point is that olive oil is one of those ingredients that rewards a bit of attention, even if it's just taking a moment to taste what you're buying rather than assuming all green bottles are created equal.

If you're standing in front of that shelf again, here's what I'd suggest: find a bottle with a harvest date, preferably from the current or previous year. Look for something in dark glass or a tin. Taste it if you can—it should smell fresh and fruity, maybe a little grassy, and it should have a peppery kick at the back of your throat that makes you cough slightly, which is a sign of quality, not a defect. And once you've found something you like, store it somewhere cool and dark, not next to the stove where it's going to degrade faster than your will to cook after a long day at work. Good olive oil isn't cheap, but it's one of the few ingredients that can turn a plate of roasted vegetables or a slice of sourdough into something you'd actually want to eat rather than something you're eating because you told yourself you would.

You don't really need to think about vinegar this much. But once you start, it's hard to ignore how much it does with so little. Walk into any supermarket and you'll find yourself face to face with an entire aisle focused to bottles of what is essentially wine that went wrong on purpose. There's white vinegar, rice vinegar, apple cider vinegar, balsamic vinegar, and half a dozen others, each one promising to transform your cooking in ways that sound vaguely medicinal. And the thing is, some of them actually deliver.

Vinegar is what happens when alcohol meets oxygen and a particular strain of bacteria called acetobacter decides to get involved. The alcohol ferments into acetic acid, which is what gives vinegar that sharp, puckering quality that makes your face do things you didn't authorise. Different starting materials produce different vinegars, which is why rice vinegar tastes nothing like the malt vinegar you'd find drowning a pile of fish and chips in some rain-soaked corner of Britain.

Perhaps the most stripped-back way to see what vinegar actually does is to start with white vinegar, which is distilled from grain alcohol and ends up tasting like pure, undiluted sharpness with nothing else to soften the blow. It's the sort of thing you'd use to clean your kitchen surfaces or pickle vegetables that need to be shocked into submission. You wouldn't exactly want to dress a salad with it unless you were trying to prove some sort of point about minimalism. But for brining, for cutting through grease, or for adding a jolt of acidity to a pot of beans that's gone a bit flat, white vinegar does the job with the subtlety of a fire alarm.

Rice vinegar, on the other hand, is what happens when you take that same principle and soften it considerably. Made from fermented rice, it's mild, faintly sweet, and the sort of thing that shows up in sushi rice, dipping sauces, and stir-fries across East Asia. The Chinese have been making it for thousands of years, which gives you some sense of how fundamental it is to the region's cooking. There's also black rice vinegar, which is darker, richer, and has a malty depth that works particularly well in braised dishes or as a dipping sauce for dumplings. You'll find it in Zhenjiang vinegar, which is the sort of thing that quietly holds together half the dishes in a dim sum spread without anyone giving it much credit.

Apple cider vinegar has spent the last decade being promoted as a miracle tonic that supposedly does everything from improving digestion to making your hair shinier, which is the sort of claim that makes you wonder if anyone's actually tasted it straight. It's made from fermented apple juice and has a fruity, slightly funky quality that can work well in salad dressings or marinades, but drinking it undiluted is an experience best described as regrettable. That said, it does have a roundness that white vinegar lacks, and if you're making a vinaigrette, it brings a gentle sweetness that pairs well with mustard and olive oil.

Then we have balsamic vinegar, which is where things get expensive and slightly pretentious. Traditional balsamic vinegar from Modena is aged for years in wooden barrels, developing a thick, syrupy consistency and a flavour that's equal parts sweet, tangy, and complex. It's the sort of thing you'd drizzle over strawberries or a wedge of Parmigiano-Reggiano, not the sort of thing you'd use to clean your countertops. The cheaper stuff you find in supermarkets is often just wine vinegar with added colouring and sweeteners, which does a decent enough job if you're tossing a salad but won't give you that same slow-building richness. If you can see a price tag that makes you wince slightly, you're probably looking at the real thing.

Malt vinegar is what the British have been pouring over their chips for as long as anyone can remember, and it's made from fermented barley. It has a robust, slightly sweet flavour that works well with fried foods, partly because it cuts through the grease and partly because it just tastes right in that context. You wouldn't use it in a delicate Vietnamese dipping sauce, but for pickling onions or giving a bit of sharpness to a meat pie, it does exactly what it's supposed to do.

Sherry vinegar is Spain's contribution to the vinegar world, and it's aged in a solera system, which means younger vinegar is blended with older batches over time. This gives it a nutty, slightly caramelised quality that works particularly well in gazpacho or drizzled over roasted vegetables. It's less sharp than wine vinegar and has a warmth that makes it feel almost comforting, which is not a word you'd normally associate with something that's essentially controlled spoilage.

Of course, I could keep going. There's champagne vinegar, red wine vinegar, coconut vinegar, and all manner of other variants that would require an entire book if we were to cover them properly. The point is that vinegar isn't just one thing, and treating it as interchangeable is the kind of oversight that can quietly sabotage a dish without you realising what went wrong.

If you needed any more convincing, start paying attention to which vinegar you're reaching for and see how it changes what you're cooking. A splash of rice vinegar in a stir-fry will brighten it without overpowering the other flavours. A drizzle of aged balsamic over roasted tomatoes will make them taste like they've been concentrating their efforts for hours. A bit of apple cider vinegar in a slow-cooked stew will lift the entire thing without announcing its presence. Vinegar does a lot of heavy lifting in the kitchen, and it does it quietly, which is probably why most people don't think about it until something tastes a bit off and they can't figure out why.

Have you ever wondered why a bowl of ramen tastes infinitely more satisfying than the sum of its parts, or why a well-aged Parmesan can make you close your eyes and forget where you are for a moment? A simple enough question, sure, but one with an answer far stranger than you might expect.

You're probably looking at me like I've lost my mind. Umami's just another taste, right? Like sweet or salty, except with a fancier name that makes it sound like something you'd order at a trendy café. Point to the MSG in your instant noodles, wave vaguely at a piece of kombu, and call it a day. Except that's not quite how it works, and the mechanism behind what umami actually does to your tongue—and your brain—is equal parts fascinating and slightly unnerving (in the best possible way).

Let's start with glutamate, the amino acid responsible for triggering the umami taste. Glutamate exists naturally in all sorts of foods: tomatoes, mushrooms, aged cheeses, cured meats, seaweed, soy sauce, fish sauce. When it hits specific receptors on your tongue—T1R1 and T1R3, if you're keeping score at home—those receptors send a signal to your brain that essentially says, "This contains protein. This is good. Eat more of this." It's a neurological sleight of hand that evolved to help us identify nutrient-dense foods, and it works so well that we've spent centuries figuring out how to concentrate it, bottle it, and sprinkle it on everything from crisps to instant ramen.

Then there's inosinate, found in high concentrations in bonito flakes, dried anchovies, and meat. On its own, inosinate doesn't do much. Pair it with glutamate, though, and something remarkable happens: the two compounds amplify each other in what scientists call a synergistic effect. A dashi made from kombu and katsuobushi isn't just the sum of two ingredients—it's a multiplicative explosion of savouriness that punches well above its weight. The Japanese worked this out centuries ago without needing a lab coat or a research grant, and the rest of the world has been playing catch-up ever since.

Guanylate operates on a similar principle. You'll find it in dried shiitake mushrooms and certain vegetables, and like inosinate, it teams up with glutamate to create a flavour intensity that neither compound can achieve alone. This is why a mushroom risotto finished with Parmesan tastes so much richer than you'd expect from something that's ostensibly just rice, fungi, and cheese. The guanylate in the mushrooms and the glutamate in the Parmesan are having a conversation your taste buds can't ignore, and the result is the kind of dish that makes you scrape the bottom of the bowl with a spoon and wonder if it's socially acceptable to lick it clean.

What's particularly clever about umami—and what sets it apart from the other four tastes—is that it doesn't just register as a flavour. It also triggers salivation and increases the perception of richness and body in a dish, which is why umami-heavy foods feel more substantial and satisfying even when they're not particularly calorie-dense. A clear broth simmered with kombu and bonito can taste as deeply fulfilling as a cream-based soup, not because it's thicker or heavier, but because your brain has been tricked into thinking it's just consumed something profoundly nourishing. It's ingenious trickery, really, and it's the reason why a well-made dashi feels like it's wrapping you in a warm blanket even though it's mostly water.

Of course, none of this is to say that umami is some kind of magic bullet that elevates every dish it touches. Like any other taste, it needs balance. Too much glutamate without the right supporting cast of salt, acid, and sweetness, and you're left with a one-dimensional savouriness that clings to your palate in all the wrong ways. But when it's deployed with precision—a dash of fish sauce in a caramel, a spoonful of miso stirred into a vinaigrette, a scattering of Parmesan over roasted vegetables—it does something remarkable: it makes food taste more like itself, as if someone turned up the volume on flavours that were already there but just needed a little nudge to come forward.

So yes, umami is a constructed category, a label we invented to describe a taste that humans have been chasing for millennia without quite knowing what to call it. But that doesn't make it any less real, and it certainly doesn't make it any less useful. We've agreed that this particular sensation—this mouth-coating, appetite-triggering, deeply satisfying taste—deserves its own name and its own place at the table. And honestly, given how much pleasure it brings, that has to count for something.

Instant coffee isn't bad. It's just misunderstood by people who've decided it's beneath them without ever really thinking about what it is or why it exists.

Now you might be thinking I've lost the plot entirely, what with all the specialty cafés dotting Kuala Lumpur like particularly pretentious mushrooms after rain, but hear me out. The stuff gets dismissed as a sad compromise, the thing you reach for when you can't be bothered to do coffee properly, but that's a gross simplification of what's actually a rather ingenious bit of food science that's been keeping people caffeinated since the early 1900s. You point to your freshly ground beans and your pour-over setup and I'll point to the jar of Nescafé that's probably sitting in your parents' kitchen right now, doing its job without complaint.

Take freeze-dried instant coffee, which is what you're getting if you're buying anything halfway decent. The process starts with brewed coffee that's been concentrated, then frozen at temperatures low enough to make your home freezer look like a sauna. What happens next is sublimation, which is just a fancy way of saying the ice crystals turn directly into vapour without passing through the liquid stage first. It's the same principle that makes ice cubes shrink in your freezer over time, except this is done in a vacuum chamber under controlled conditions. What's left are those familiar granules that dissolve the moment hot water hits them. The whole thing was perfected by Nestlé in 1938, and while I'm not about to write love letters to multinational corporations, you have to admit that turning liquid coffee into shelf-stable powder that reconstitutes on demand is a neat trick.

Then there's spray-dried instant coffee, which takes that same concentrated brew and fires it through a hot chamber as a fine mist. The droplets dry mid-air and fall as powder. It's faster and cheaper than freeze-drying, which is why it's the method behind most budget instant coffees. The trade-off is that the high heat can damage some of the more delicate flavour compounds, leaving you with something that tastes more like the idea of coffee than actual coffee. But even that has its place. Sometimes you just want caffeine and warmth in a cup, and there's no shame in that (hopefully).

The real problem isn't that instant coffee tastes bad. It's that we've been sold a story about what coffee should be, and instant doesn't fit the narrative. Specialty coffee culture has done wonderful things for appreciation of the bean and the craft of extraction, but it's also created a kind of snobbery that treats instant as the enemy. Walk into any kopitiam in Malaysia and you'll find people drinking instant coffee mixed with condensed milk, completely unbothered by the fact that it came from a packet. It's sweet, it's strong, and it gets the job done. Much like the city itself, nobody's pretending it's something it's not.

Of course, not all instant coffee is created equal. The freeze-dried stuff from brands like Wonda or the higher-end Nescafé Gold tastes noticeably better than the cheapest options, mostly because the process preserves more of the original coffee's character. Single-origin instant coffees have started appearing too, which is a strange sentence to write but there you go. These tend to come from specialty roasters who've realised there's a market for people who want convenience without sacrificing flavour entirely. They cost more, naturally, but if you're the sort who cares about where your beans come from, at least the option exists.

Here's what it comes down to: instant coffee is a tool. It's not trying to replace your morning ritual of grinding beans and timing your bloom phase and whatever else you do to feel alive before nine in the morning. It's there for the times when you need coffee and you need it now, or when you're travelling and the hotel kettle is your only option, or when you're at your parents' place and they're offering you a cup from the jar they've had since 2015. In those moments, instant coffee does exactly what it's supposed to do. It gives you something warm, caffeinated, and recognisable as coffee.

So maybe it's time we stopped treating it like a guilty secret. I'm not suggesting you ditch your pour-over setup or cancel your subscription to that roaster in Penang who hand-delivers beans on a vintage bicycle (shall we say, an admirably committed bit of branding). I'm just saying there's room for both. The world's complicated enough without adding coffee snobbery to the mix. If someone offers you instant, just drink it. It won't kill you, and it might even taste better than you remember.

That burning sensation in your mouth isn't heat at all, but a neurological sleight of hand courtesy of a compound called capsaicin that's very good at lying to your nerves. Have you ever wondered what makes your food spicy? A simple enough question, sure, but one with an answer far stranger than you might expect. Despite your tingling tongue and watering eyes telling you otherwise, what registers as heat when you bite into a chilli pepper isn't actually a taste at all. In fact, it's a reaction of the body that is not very different from if you'd pressed your hand against a hot stove. The culprit behind this apparent malfunction of your senses is a compound called capsaicin, which is primarily found in the white membranes and pith inside chilli peppers. Capsaicin works by binding to something known as TRPV1 receptors, which are heat-sensitive nerve receptors that normally exist to warn you when something is genuinely hot enough to cause harm. Capsaicin, through a most fascinating mechanism of action, essentially hijacks this system. . What then follows is that hellishly familiar sensation that leaves you sweating, turning red as a beet, and reaching for the nearest glass of water (or milk, if you're smarter than the average bear). Your mouth might not be on fire, but your nervous system, through capsaicin's ingenious trickery, has been led to believe otherwise. Of course, not all chillies are created equal, and to measure just how much capsaicin a pepper contains, we use something called the Scoville Scale, named after the American pharmacist Wilbur Scoville. Measured in Scoville Heat Units, or SHU, the scale ranges from things like bell peppers, which sit comfortably at zero, meaning no heat, to peppers with a little bit of kick to them like jalapeños that range between 2,500 and 8,000 SHU, to tear-inducing aberrations like habaneros, which can reach anywhere from 100,000 to 350,000. . As if this wasn't enough, there is a brand of absolute sadists who have dedicated years of their lives towards the express goal of making peppers even hotter. Through selective breeding, growers have crossed particularly fiery varieties over multiple generations in an effort to increase capsaicin concentrations. . Among them are names you've probably heard before, such as the Carolina Reaper or Pepper X, the current incumbent atop the infernal throne of the world's hottest pepper. These peppers are so potent that eating them has become something of a competitive sport, though why anybody would willingly subject themselves to such an experience is beyond me. It is, when you stop to think about it, a rather absurd state of affairs. A pepper evolves to stop things from eating it, and what do we do? We hold competitions to see who can eat the most aggressive version of it. You don't really need to think about salt this much. But once you start, it becomes impossible to ignore how much work it's doing while pretending to be simple. Walk into any supermarket and you'll find yourself staring at an entire shelf of what is, chemically speaking, the same compound arranged in different shapes and sold at wildly different prices. There's the standard table salt in the blue container that's been in your grandmother's kitchen since before you were born, flaky sea salt that costs more per gram than some cuts of meat, and pink Himalayan salt being marketed like it fell from some mystical mountain peak where enlightenment grows on trees. And the thing is, some of these differences actually matter.

Salt's place in cooking goes back far enough that trying to pinpoint an origin feels about as useful as asking when humans first figured out that fire was warm. What we do know is that salt was valuable enough to be used as currency in some parts of the world, and that entire trade routes were built around moving it from place to place. The Romans paid soldiers partly in salt—hence "salary"—and in certain parts of Africa, salt was traded weight-for-weight with gold. People have fought wars over it, taxed it, smuggled it, and generally treated it like the extremely powerful chemical seasoning that it is, long before anyone could explain why it makes food taste better.

Table salt is where most people start, and there's no shame in that. It's sodium chloride that's been mined, refined, and ground down until it's as fine as sand, with iodine added to prevent thyroid problems in populations that might not get enough of it otherwise. The iodisation programme that started in the 1920s is genuinely one of public health's quiet success stories, which doesn't get talked about much because nobody writes songs about goitre prevention. Table salt dissolves fast, measures consistently, and costs almost nothing. If you're baking and a recipe calls for salt without specifying which kind, this is probably what they mean. The grains are uniform, which makes it reliable. It's also, shall we say, not particularly interesting to look at.

Sea salt is what happens when you let ocean water evaporate and scrape up what's left behind. Unlike table salt, it hasn't been stripped of its trace minerals, which means it carries faint notes of whatever else was in that particular body of water—calcium, magnesium, potassium, and all manner of other elements that give it a slightly more complex flavour. The crystals are larger and more irregular, which means they don't dissolve as quickly and can add a bit of texture when used as a finishing salt. Maldon, the flaky pyramid-shaped stuff from England, has become something of a kitchen staple for people who like the idea of sprinkling something visibly crunchy over a dish just before serving. You'll find chefs using it on everything from chocolate chip cookies to grilled fish, where it provides a brief saline crunch before disappearing. It's also about ten times the price of table salt, which either feels justified or completely absurd depending on how much you care about the difference.

Then there's kosher salt, which gets its name not because it's kosher—all pure salt is kosher—but because its large, coarse grains were traditionally used in the koshering process to draw blood out of meat. The flaky structure makes it easy to pinch and sprinkle with your fingers, and because the crystals are larger, a tablespoon of kosher salt weighs less than a tablespoon of table salt. This is the kind of thing that sounds trivial until you accidentally oversalt something because you didn't account for it. Many professional kitchens default to kosher salt for this reason: it's easier to control, doesn't clump, and has a clean, straightforward salinity without the slightly metallic edge that iodised table salt can sometimes carry. Diamond Crystal and Morton are the two brands you'll see most often, and they're not interchangeable—Diamond Crystal's crystals are larger and lighter, so if a recipe specifies one and you use the other, you'll need to adjust.

Himalayan pink salt has become the sort of thing people either swear by or roll their eyes at, depending on how much they believe marketing copy about ancient mineral deposits. It comes from the Khewra Salt Mine in Pakistan, not the Himalayas, which is the first sign that we're dealing with some creative geography. The pink colour comes from trace amounts of iron oxide—rust, essentially—and while it does contain dozens of other minerals in tiny quantities, the amounts are so small that you'd need to eat an inadvisable quantity of salt to get any meaningful nutritional benefit from them. What it does have is a certain visual appeal. Those large pink crystals look striking in a grinder or piled in a bowl, and the flavour is clean and mild. Some people claim they can taste the difference. Most people probably can't, but that doesn't stop it from being a perfectly serviceable salt if you can see past the health claims and just appreciate it for what it is: sodium chloride with a nice colour.

Fleur de sel is the sort of salt that makes you feel like you're doing something fancy just by owning it. It's hand-harvested from the surface of salt ponds in France—traditionally in Guérande—where it forms delicate crystals that are skimmed off before they sink. The process is labour-intensive, the yield is small, and the price reflects that. What you get is a moist, slightly grey salt with a subtle minerality and a texture that feels almost soft. It's the kind of thing you use sparingly, sprinkled over a finished dish where you'll actually notice it: a perfectly seared steak, heirloom tomatoes, caramel, dark chocolate. Using fleur de sel to season pasta water would be like using a first-edition book as kindling. Technically possible, but missing the point entirely.

Having said that, I've left out smoked salts, black lava salt, sel gris, and all manner of other variations that would require me to turn this into something resembling an encyclopedia. The world of salt is far larger than most people give it credit for, and you could spend years exploring regional variations and traditional harvesting methods if you had that kind of time and a particularly high tolerance for sodium.

If there's one thing worth taking away from all this, it's that salt isn't just salt in the same way that butter isn't just butter. The differences might be subtle, but they're there, and once you start paying attention, it's hard to go back to treating it like a background ingredient that just exists. Whether you stick with the blue container or start collecting artisanal finishing salts from different coastlines is entirely up to you. But at least now you'll know what you're looking at when you find yourself standing in that supermarket aisle, staring at a dozen versions of the same chemical compound and wondering if it really matters which one you choose. Sometimes it does. Sometimes it doesn't. That has to count for something.

You don't really need to think about chocolate this much. But once you start, it becomes difficult to go back to treating it like just another sweet thing you grab at the checkout counter. Chocolate starts as cacao pods, which grow on trees that are notoriously particular about where they're willing to live. They want heat, humidity, and shade, which is why you'll find them clustered around the equator in places like West Africa, Ecuador, and parts of Southeast Asia. The pods get cracked open to reveal beans covered in a sweet, mucilaginous pulp that ferments for several days before the beans are dried, roasted, and ground into a paste. That paste is chocolate in its most fundamental form, before anyone's added sugar, milk, or vanilla to make it more agreeable to the average person's expectations of what chocolate should taste like.

Dark chocolate is what you get when you take that paste, add some sugar, and leave out the milk. The percentage you see on the wrapper refers to how much of the bar comes from cacao—the rest is sugar and maybe a bit of vanilla or lecithin to smooth things out. A 70% bar has more cacao and less sugar than a 50% bar, which means it's more bitter, more intense, and harder to eat an entire block of in one sitting (though people certainly try). Milk chocolate takes that same paste and adds milk powder, which softens everything and makes it taste like the chocolate you probably grew up with. It's sweeter, creamier, and a lot easier to eat without pausing to consider whether you're supposed to be detecting notes of red fruit or tobacco or whatever else the tasting notes on the back of the package are trying to convince you exists. Cadbury, Hershey's, and a dozen other brands have built empires on this version of chocolate, the kind that doesn't ask much of you beyond enjoying it. There's a reason it's the default.

White chocolate is technically not chocolate at all, since it contains no cacao solids—just cacao butter, sugar, and milk. This makes it the subject of endless debate among people who take chocolate seriously, though most of them will grudgingly admit that a good white chocolate has its place, particularly when it's been made with actual cacao butter rather than vegetable oil. The best white chocolate tastes like cream that's been sweetened and solidified, with a faint floral note that comes from the cacao butter itself. The worst white chocolate tastes like sweetened wax, which is probably because it is sweetened wax, or close enough to it that the distinction doesn't matter much.

Malaysian chocolate deserves its own mention here, particularly the stuff coming out of Sabah and Sarawak, where cacao has been growing quietly for years while everyone was paying attention to beans from Ecuador and Madagascar. The climate is right, the soil is right, and the people who've been working with it have figured out how to ferment and dry it in ways that bring out fruity, floral notes that feel distinctly Southeast Asian. You'll find local chocolate makers like Chocolate Concierge and Cocoraw turning these beans into bars that can hold their own against anything imported, and the fact that they're doing it here, with beans grown a few hours' drive from where you're reading this, feels like the kind of quiet achievement that should probably get more attention than it does.

Then there's the question of tempering, which is the process of heating and cooling chocolate in a very specific way so that it sets with a glossy finish and a satisfying snap when you break it. Get the temperature wrong by a few degrees and you end up with chocolate that looks dull and feels grainy, the kind that blooms with white streaks after a few weeks because the cocoa butter has separated out and crystallised on the surface. It's still edible, but it's lost that sleek, professional sheen that makes you feel like you're eating something that was made by someone who knew what they were doing. Tempering is one of those techniques that separates the casual home cook from the person who's willing to stand over a pot with a thermometer, adjusting the heat in tiny increments until the chocolate behaves itself.

I haven't even touched on single-origin bars, bean-to-bar makers, or the difference between Dutch-processed and natural cocoa powder, all of which could fill their own articles if I let them. The point isn't to become an expert overnight, or to suddenly start only buying chocolate that comes with a map of the farm where the beans were grown (though if you do, more power to you). The point is that chocolate, like butter or vinegar or any other ingredient that seems straightforward at first glance, turns out to be far more complicated once you start paying attention.

If you've been reaching for the same chocolate bar for years without thinking about it, maybe it's time to try something different. Pick up a bar with a higher percentage, or one made with beans from a specific region, or one from a Malaysian maker who's been working with local cacao. You might not taste the notes of caramel and blackcurrant that the packaging promises, but you'll probably taste something, and that has to count for something.

There's no magic in sourdough. There, I said it. The entire mystique around it—the talk of ancient traditions, the reverent whispers about mother starters passed down through generations, the Instagram accounts focused to scoring patterns—it's all a bit much when you consider what's actually happening. You're feeding flour and water to wild yeast and bacteria, and they're burping out gas. That's it. That's the miracle everyone's been going on about.

Now, you're probably looking at me like I've just told you Santa isn't real (he's not, by the way, but that's another article). You point to your starter jar, bubbling away on the counter, and you say, "But Colin, I've been nurturing this for weeks. I've named it. It has a personality." And I believe you. I've met people who talk to their starters. I've read forum posts where grown adults debate whether their starter prefers jazz or classical music. But the thing doing the actual work—the rising, the flavour, the structure—doesn't care about any of that. It cares about having something to eat and a place that's not too cold or too hot. The rest is just us projecting.

Take wheat flour, for instance. When you mix it with water, you're not making dough yet—you're making a buffet. The flour contains starches, and those starches are just long chains of sugar molecules waiting to be broken down. Your sourdough starter, which is really just a colony of wild yeast (mostly Saccharomyces exiguus, if you want to get specific) and lactic acid bacteria (Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis being the famous one), gets to work immediately. The bacteria produce enzymes that chop those starch chains into simple sugars. The yeast then gobbles up those sugars and, in a process called fermentation, converts them into carbon dioxide and ethanol. The carbon dioxide gets trapped in the gluten network you've been developing through all that kneading and folding, and that's what makes your dough rise. The ethanol mostly bakes off in the oven, though it does contribute a bit to the flavour. It's a symbiotic relationship that's been happening since ancient Egypt, though I doubt the Egyptians were posting about it on social media.

Then there's the question of why it takes so long. Commercial yeast—the stuff you buy in those little packets—is a single, highly efficient strain of Saccharomyces cerevisiae that's been bred to work fast. It's the thoroughbred of the yeast world. Your sourdough starter, by contrast, is more like a small village. There are multiple species of yeast and bacteria in there, all working at different speeds, all producing slightly different byproducts. The lactic acid bacteria produce lactic acid (hence the name, in a rare moment of scientific clarity), which gives sourdough its characteristic tang. Some strains also produce acetic acid, which adds a sharper, more vinegary note. This slower fermentation is why sourdough takes hours or even days to rise properly, and it's also why the flavour is so much more complex than what you get from commercial yeast. The microbes are basically having a very slow dinner party in your dough, and the longer they stay, the more interesting the conversation (flavour) becomes.

But here's where it gets oddly specific: temperature matters more than almost anything else. Your starter is most active between 24°C and 28°C. Below that, the yeast slows down. Above that, the bacteria start to dominate, and you get a more acidic loaf. This is why bakers who know what they're doing will proof their dough in the fridge overnight—the cold slows everything down, giving the bacteria time to develop flavour without producing so much acid that the dough turns into a science experiment gone wrong. It's also why your friend who insists their apartment is "the perfect temperature for sourdough" probably just has a flat that hovers around 26°C (or they're delusional, which is equally likely).

And then we have hydration, which is just baker-speak for how much water you've added relative to flour. A higher hydration dough (say, 75% water to flour) will ferment faster than a stiffer one because the microbes can move around more easily. It'll also be stickier and harder to handle, which is why every sourdough tutorial on the internet makes it look effortless while your own attempts end up resembling something that should be scraped off a pavement. The gluten network in a high-hydration dough is more extensible, meaning it can trap more gas and give you those big, irregular holes that make people think you know what you're doing. A lower hydration dough (60% or so) will rise more slowly, but it'll be easier to shape and will give you a tighter crumb. Neither is better. They're just different. Though if you're the sort of person who needs your bread to look like it belongs in a museum, you'll want to go higher.

So what does all this say about us, really? We've taken a process that's fundamentally about controlled rot—microbes breaking down starches and proteins, producing acid and gas as waste products—and turned it into a marker of sophistication. We've convinced ourselves that there's virtue in waiting three days for a loaf of bread when the supermarket will sell you one for a few ringgit. We've built entire identities around our ability to keep a jar of flour and water alive on the counter. And you know what? That's fine. Humans have always been good at taking the mundane and making it mean something. Money is just paper until we all agree it's worth something. Take kimchi, for instance. You salt some cabbage, pack it into a jar, and leave it on your counter for a few days. What's happening in there is that Lactobacillus bacteria—the same stuff that lives in your gut, shall we say, harmoniously—are eating the sugars in the vegetables and producing lactic acid as waste. That acid drops the pH low enough that the bacteria you don't want (the ones that would turn your cabbage into a science experiment gone wrong) can't survive. It's not preservation through technique. It's preservation through making the environment so inhospitable that only the good microbes stick around. The Koreans figured this out centuries before anyone knew what bacteria were, which is either impressive or slightly concerning depending on how you feel about trial and error in food safety.

Or consider beer, which is just controlled spoilage with carbonation. You take grain, soak it until it starts to sprout, dry it out, crush it, boil it with hops, and then introduce yeast that converts the sugars into alcohol and carbon dioxide. The yeast—usually Saccharomyces cerevisiae if you're brewing ales, or Saccharomyces pastorianus for lagers—is doing exactly what it would do in the wild, which is eat sugar and excrete alcohol as a byproduct. We've just learned to time it, control the temperature, and bottle the results before it turns into vinegar. Every beer you've ever had is the result of billions of microscopic organisms getting drunk on malt and dying in the process. That's not poetry. That's just biology with better branding.

Then there's cheese, which starts as milk and ends as something that can age for years without refrigeration. You add bacteria or mould (Penicillium roqueforti for blue cheese, Propionibacterium freudenreichii for the holes in Swiss), and they break down the lactose and proteins into smaller compounds that taste complex and nutty instead of, well, like milk. Some cheeses are aged in caves where the humidity and temperature stay consistent year-round. Others are washed in brine or alcohol to encourage specific bacteria to grow on the rind. The process is so specific that certain cheeses can only be made in certain regions because the local microbes are part of the flavour profile. Roquefort legally has to be aged in the caves of Roquefort-sur-Soulzon in France. Not because of tradition, but because the Penicillium roqueforti that lives in those caves is different from the stuff anywhere else. You're not eating cheese. You're eating geography.

Even soy sauce—the bottle sitting in your kitchen right now—is fermented. You take soybeans, mix them with roasted wheat, introduce Aspergillus oryzae (a mould), let it grow for a few days, then pack the whole thing into brine and leave it for months or years. It's the whole point.

What all of this points to is that fermentation isn't a technique we invented. It's a process we borrowed from nature and learned to control just enough to make it useful. The bacteria and yeast were already breaking down sugars and producing acids and alcohol long before humans showed up. We just started putting it in jars and calling it food. The real skill isn't in the fermentation itself—it's in creating the conditions where the right organisms thrive and the wrong ones don't. Salt, temperature, time, and sometimes a little bit of luck.
